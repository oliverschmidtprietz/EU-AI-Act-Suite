# Annex III Area 4 — Employment, workers management, access to self-employment

**Annex III citation:** Nr. 4
**Effective date:** 2 December 2027 (per AI Omnibus)
**Source:** Commission Guidelines Annex III, Area 4 section. Full text: [../../ai-act-knowledge/references/commission-guidelines/art-6-classification-annex-iii-2026.md](../../ai-act-knowledge/references/commission-guidelines/art-6-classification-annex-iii-2026.md).

> **High-priority area.** CV-screening, candidate ranking, and performance-monitoring AI are the canonical Annex III examples. This file includes extra worked examples and explicit guidance on the profiling re-exception, which almost always applies here.

## Scope of the area

Annex III Nr. 4 captures AI systems used across the **full employment lifecycle**: from the placement of job advertisements and sourcing of candidates, through application analysis, shortlisting and selection, onboarding (where outcome-affecting), task allocation and work assignment, performance and behaviour monitoring, decisions on terms of work (pay, working time, leave), through to promotion and termination of work-related relationships. Recital 57 makes explicit that these systems can have "an appreciable impact on future career prospects, livelihoods of those persons and workers' rights" and "may perpetuate historical patterns of discrimination" against women, age groups, persons with disabilities, racial or ethnic minorities, and persons of certain sexual orientation.

The **personal scope** is deliberately broad. "Work-related relationships" reaches well beyond formal employment contracts: it covers employees, persons providing services through platforms, independent contractors, freelancers, solo self-employed persons in situations comparable to workers, and any arrangement where an AI system **mediates or conditions access to work opportunities**. The notion of "workplace" is likewise interpreted broadly to include any physical or virtual space where work is performed.

The rationale is the **structural power imbalance** between employer/contracting party and worker/candidate, the high information asymmetry, and the fact that employment decisions materially affect economic livelihood, dignity, fundamental rights, data protection and privacy. Crucially, Annex III Nr. 4 also operates **alongside the Art. 5(1)(f) prohibition** on emotion recognition in the workplace and educational settings: where both apply, the prohibition prevails and the system is unlawful regardless of safeguards.

## Sub-categories

- **Nr. 4(a) — Recruitment and selection** — including placement of targeted job ads, application filtering, candidate evaluation, ranking. Covers preparatory steps (advertising, prospecting, pre-application screening) and the selection process (shortlisting, grading, ranking, testing). Includes self-employment selection and onboarding where the outcome affects access to work.
- **Nr. 4(b) — Workers management** — promotion/termination decisions, decisions on terms of work-related relationships, task allocation by individual behaviour/traits, monitoring/evaluating performance and behaviour of workers and persons in work-related relationships.

## Practical examples — HIGH-RISK

### Nr. 4(a) Recruitment and selection
- **Automated job-matching and candidate ranking tool.** Processes structured and unstructured data (CVs, skills, education, past placements, cognitive/social/emotional competencies), compares to job descriptions or historical hiring data, generates scores and rankings ("top 5 candidates") or fit categories ("high fit", "low fit"). Outputs serve as primary input to shortlisting; recruiter retains formal discretion but is heavily influenced.
- **Candidate sourcing across online platforms.** Recruitment agency tool that scrapes social media, professional sites, job boards and internal CV databases against recruiter-defined criteria (years of experience, technical skills, education) and generates a shortlist of natural persons.
- **AI ranking self-employed service providers.** Online platform's job-matching tool that ranks self-employed providers and decides which are presented to potential consumers — directly conditioning access to future assignments.
- **AI vision-monitoring system for pilot recruitment.** Camera and sensor system that assesses visual acuity and reaction speed of pilot candidates and determines eligibility for long-haul vs. short-haul flights.
- **AI scoring of applicant responses in online assessments.** Scores written or oral answers (including avatar-based video interviews) and ranks applicants for invitation to the next stage.
- **AI supporting apprenticeship recruitment.** Manages and screens apprenticeship applications, matches applicants to roles, generates shortlists based on qualifications or age.
- **AI placing targeted job advertisements on social media.** Analyses navigation patterns and user characteristics; advertisers supply targeting criteria (age, education, sector); the system identifies and prioritises audiences who become aware of vacancies.
- **AI used by employment agencies to assign candidates to vacancies.** Processes CV data, skills, education, employment history against job requirements and labour-market data; ranks candidates per vacancy or suggests vacancies per jobseeker; caseworkers rely heavily on outputs.
- **AI background-check tool for job applicants.** Aggregates official records, employment history, social network history, financial/credit data (where lawful), open-source data; outputs composite risk scores or "low/medium/high risk" categories that filter candidates before human review.
- **AI generating qualification requirements + evaluating candidates against them.** A job-description generator that itself defines the necessary qualifications/skills and then evaluates CVs against the description it created and recommends suitability.

### Nr. 4(b) Workers management
- **AI scheduler in retail/logistics assigning shifts, rest periods and on-call windows.** Uses punctuality, no-show history, acceptance/decline rates, customer/manager ratings; ranks workers per slot; auto-allocates priority shifts to high performers; downgrades workers who decline "priority offers". Affects pay, progression, predictability.
- **Tutor performance scoring with auto-suspension.** Online tutoring platform aggregates student satisfaction, lesson completion, punctuality logs into a "tutor performance score"; if below 4/5 for three consecutive weeks, the system automatically suspends the account, with permanent deactivation on continued low ratings — functionally a termination.
- **Workload allocation in a law firm.** System ingests billing hours, turnaround times, email responsiveness, voluntary participation in firm activities; aggregates into a score that drives allocation of client matters — task allocation on individual behaviour.
- **Dynamic pricing and pay determination in platform work.** Ride-hailing platform sets per-ride fares and individual pay multipliers based on demand, driver acceptance rates, passenger ratings, completion times; lower-rated drivers receive reduced pay coefficients.
- **AI assignment of civil servants to posts.** Public administration allocates teachers/doctors to specific posts after a competition exam, using test scores, geographic preferences, vacancies, seniority; output is decisive in practice.
- **Individual-level keystroke / productivity monitoring.** Tracks individual worker keystrokes, mouse activity, screen time, application switches and produces individual productivity scores feeding into appraisals or pay.
- **AI ranking self-employed external lawyers** from a pool based on response time and ratings from internal counsel — task allocation conditioned on behavioural profiling of natural persons.
- **AI withholding delivery slots** from couriers with low acceptance rates or slow response times — conditioning work opportunities on behavioural profiling.
- **AI evaluating performance in mandatory training** where completion conditions promotion, pay grade or continued employability.

## Practical examples — NOT HIGH-RISK

- **Non-inclusive wording detector for job ads.** Flags discriminatory or non-inclusive phrasing in ad copy using human-curated patterns; not intended to recruit or select natural persons.
- **Employer brand advertisements** highlighting workplace culture, benefits, career development — not tied to a specific vacancy, non-discriminatory.
- **Employer reputation monitoring.** Scans public comments and reviews about the employer in aggregate; no analysis of specific users (data anonymised at collection).
- **Employee onboarding assistant** providing personalised information about company policies, training schedules, FAQs — supports internal HR after hiring, does not influence recruitment or workers' management decisions.
- **CV-tailoring assistant used by candidates** to adapt their own CVs to specific positions — initiated and managed by the candidate, outside the employer's control.
- **Career-discovery tool for candidates** ranking job vacancies based on the candidate's own skills and preferences — candidate-controlled, outside the recruitment process.
- **Delivery-tracking system** that monitors parcels in transit and flags routing deviations to the responsible employee for correction — incidental monitoring of workflow, not worker evaluation (provided it is not used for performance assessment).
- **Voluntary training-progress feedback tool** that provides learning recommendations to the employee only — not appraising job performance.
- **Office space booking optimiser** matching desk reservations with availability — does not allocate tasks, evaluate workers, or affect employment rights.
- **Corporate travel planner** suggesting cost-effective flights/hotels for business travel — supports logistics, no employment-decision effect.
- **Delivery-area suggestions to platform couriers** ("high demand in Area X 1–2pm") with no penalty for ignoring and no record of acceptance/refusal — non-binding informational support.
- **Payroll automation, expense processing, tax withholding** — purely transactional administrative processing.
- **Generic productivity tools** (calendars, document editors, project trackers) where the AI does not evaluate workers individually.
- **Aggregate workforce analytics** (e.g. team-level attrition forecasting) where no individual-level evaluation is produced and no individual decisions are taken.
- **Compliance-driven monitoring** (e.g. transaction logging for financial market-abuse rules) deployed *exclusively* to meet external legal/regulatory obligations and not used for performance evaluation.
- **Medical-/safety- or property-protection monitoring** deployed purely for those purposes (e.g. CCTV for theft, biometric access control, fatigue detection for safety) and not used to evaluate worker performance.

## Common Art. 6(3) exception scenarios

The general decision tree lives in [art-6-3-exception-decision-tree.md](art-6-3-exception-decision-tree.md). For Area 4, the following limbs sometimes apply — but read the **profiling caveat below** first.

- **(a) Narrow procedural task** — Examples accepted by the Commission Guidelines:
  - Verifying professional accreditations of applicants against an official registry (e.g. bar membership number) with binary "confirmed"/"not confirmed" output.
  - Recognising and organising CV information into a searchable internal database for recruiters (no scoring or filtering).
  - Interview scheduling automation (calendar integration, time-zone matching, accessibility-needs intake).
  - Sending personalised acknowledgement emails to applicants.

- **(b) Improvement of completed human activity** — Example accepted by the Guidelines:
  - AI writing assistant that refines managers' **already-completed** promotion evaluations (recommendation, rating and justification all decided by the manager first); AI only polishes language, ensures consistency, flags potentially biased wording. Internal policy requires the manager to double-check the revised text.

- **(c) Detection of decision-making patterns** — Example accepted by the Guidelines:
  - Retrospective audit of past hiring decisions on **anonymised** data to detect bias patterns; does not influence ongoing recruitment or evaluate current candidates.

- **(d) Preparatory task** — Examples accepted by the Guidelines:
  - Compiling timekeeping records into structured monthly attendance dashboards for managers (no scoring, no flagging, no evaluation).
  - Descriptive analysis of applications such as file-format conversion or classification of degrees into standard categories without evaluative weight.

**IMPORTANT — the profiling re-exception almost always blocks the exception here.** Article 6(3) last subparagraph: an AI system performing profiling of natural persons is **always high-risk** and **cannot benefit from the Art. 6(3) filter**, even if it would otherwise satisfy one of the four limbs. In Area 4, this is the dominant pattern:

- Any CV evaluation that infers skills, fit, suitability, potential, reliability or competence from personal data is **profiling**.
- Any performance or behaviour evaluation of a worker (productivity, punctuality, responsiveness, engagement) based on automated processing of personal data is **profiling**.
- Any targeted job-ad placement that uses individual user characteristics (age, education, browsing history) to decide who sees the ad is **profiling**.
- Background checks producing composite risk scores or risk categories from personal data are **profiling**.

Practical rule: in Area 4 the Art. 6(3) exception is realistically available only for (i) purely logistical/administrative AI with no evaluative content (scheduling, formatting, acknowledgement emails, accreditation lookups), (ii) post-hoc audits on anonymised data, or (iii) language polishing on already-decided human evaluations. Anything that scores, ranks, filters, monitors or evaluates a natural person → high-risk.

## Common profiling considerations

**Profiling** is defined by Art. 4(4) GDPR (referenced by Art. 3(52) AI Act) as any form of automated processing of personal data to evaluate certain personal aspects of a natural person — in particular to analyse or predict aspects concerning that person's performance at work, economic situation, health, personal preferences, interests, reliability, behaviour, location or movements.

**Recruitment screening** that evaluates a candidate's fit, skills, potential, suitability, reliability or any other personal aspect = profiling. → triggers the re-exception → system remains high-risk.

**Performance monitoring** that evaluates a worker's productivity, behaviour, punctuality, engagement, reliability or any individual attribute = profiling. → triggers the re-exception → system remains high-risk.

**Targeted job advertising** that personalises who sees the ad based on individual user characteristics (age, education, browsing patterns) = profiling. → triggers the re-exception → system remains high-risk, regardless of any other Art. 6(3) limb that might apply.

In contrast, the following are **generally NOT profiling** and may legitimately benefit from Art. 6(3) limb (c) or (d):

- Aggregate workforce analytics with no individual-level output (e.g. team-level attrition forecasts, department-level engagement trends).
- Retrospective bias-detection audits on properly anonymised historical data.
- Contextual job-ad placement (advertising based on the content of the website where the ad is shown, not on individual user characteristics).
- Logistical tooling that processes data factually without producing evaluative judgements about persons.

## Worked verdict examples

- **CV-screener with skill extraction + candidate ranking** → HIGH-RISK under Nr. 4(a). Profiling is unavoidable (evaluates candidate's personal aspects against job criteria). Art. 6(3) exception unavailable.
- **AI recruiter chatbot that pre-screens by qualifications** → HIGH-RISK if it scores, ranks or filters candidates; profiling re-exception applies. Only escapes scope if it merely confirms factual credentials (binary yes/no) and performs no evaluation.
- **AI tool polishing recruiter-written interview notes** → likely NOT high-risk under Art. 6(3)(b) (improvement of completed human activity) **if** the human assessment is fully completed first and the AI does not evaluate personal aspects. Treat as borderline; flag for assurance.
- **AI workforce planning tool predicting attrition by team** → NOT high-risk if output is aggregate-only and not used for individual decisions. Becomes HIGH-RISK if it identifies specific individuals at flight risk.
- **AI monitoring keystrokes / productivity at individual level** → HIGH-RISK under Nr. 4(b). Profiling almost certain. Art. 6(3) exception unavailable.
- **AI scheduler that allocates shifts purely on declared availability and geography** → NOT high-risk (objective, neutral, external criteria; no profiling). Becomes HIGH-RISK the moment it factors in acceptance rates, ratings or behavioural signals.
- **AI flagging biased language in job-ad drafts** → NOT high-risk; not intended for recruitment/selection of natural persons.
- **AI that drafts a job description from a recruiter-defined list of required tasks and skills** → falls under Art. 6(3)(a) narrow procedural task; NOT high-risk **unless** the AI also generates the qualification criteria itself or evaluates candidates against the description it created (then HIGH-RISK).
- **Background check producing "low/medium/high risk" labels** → HIGH-RISK; profiling.
- **Interview scheduling assistant** (calendar coordination, accessibility-needs intake, reminders) → NOT high-risk under Art. 6(3)(a) narrow procedural task.
- **Acknowledgement-email generator** that personalises greetings to candidates → NOT high-risk under Art. 6(3)(a).
- **AI that automatically suspends platform accounts based on performance score** → HIGH-RISK under Nr. 4(b); functional termination of the work relationship.
- **Art. 25(1)(c) trap example:** an enterprise deploys a general-purpose LLM with a system prompt to evaluate CVs or rank candidates → the enterprise becomes a **PROVIDER** of a high-risk AI system, with full provider obligations (CE marking, conformity assessment, technical documentation, QMS, registration). Route to `ai-act-roles`. See [art-25-substantial-modification-flag.md](art-25-substantial-modification-flag.md).
