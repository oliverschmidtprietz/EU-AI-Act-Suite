## Introduction

AI regulatory sandboxes are an important part of the implementation of the EU AI Act. According to Article 57, each Member State must establish at least one AI regulatory sandbox at the national level by 2 August 2026.

AI regulatory sandboxes create controlled environments where AI systems can be developed and tested with regulatory guidance before market release. They improve legal certainty, support compliance, allow for processing of personal data, and facilitate market access for SMEs and startups. Importantly, the documentation from participating in a sandbox can be used to demonstrate compliance with the AI Act. Further, providers will not face administrative fines for infringements of the Act, as long as they follow the guidance of the national competent authority. Providers remain liable for damages to third parties caused by experimentation with AI systems in a sandbox.

Lessons from other fields highlight some potential positive impacts. For instance, companies that completed successful testing within the UK FCA sandbox received 6.6 times more fintech investment than their peers. The UK FCA sandbox reduced the average time required for market authorisation by 40% compared to the regulator's typical approval process.

## Quick Summary of AI Regulatory Sandboxes Under Articles 57-59

- AI regulatory sandboxes are frameworks for testing AI systems in controlled environments that foster innovation and facilitate development, training, testing, and validation before market entry.
- Sandboxes aim to improve legal certainty, support sharing of best practices, foster innovation, contribute to evidence-based regulatory learning, and facilitate market access, particularly for SMEs and startups. Providers may use documentation from participating in a sandbox to demonstrate their compliance with the EU AI Act.
- Each Member State must establish at least one AI regulatory sandbox by 2 August 2026. The national sandbox may also be done jointly with other Member States.
- National competent authorities provide guidance, supervision, and support to identify risks and ensure compliance with the AI Act and other relevant legislation.
- Providers participating in sandboxes remain liable under applicable liability laws but are protected from administrative fines if they follow sandbox guidelines in good faith.
- Providers may process personal data in sandboxes for projects serving the public interest if the data is necessary, kept secure, not shared externally, and deleted after use. They must manage risks, document activities, and publish a summary unless sensitive law enforcement data is involved.
- National competent authorities must coordinate their activities through the AI Board and submit annual reports on sandbox implementation.
- SMEs and startups can access AI sandboxes free of charge, though national authorities may recover fair and proportionate exceptional costs.

## EU-Wide Support Initiatives

### The EU Regulatory Sandboxes for AI (EUSAiR)

One of the key initiatives is the EU Regulatory Sandboxes for AI (EUSAiR), a two-year project funded by the European Union's Digital Europe programme working in cooperation with the AI Office. EUSAiR aims to support the implementation of AI regulatory sandboxes by developing common frameworks, enhancing technical and legal capacities, and promoting collaboration among Member States. It aims to provide broad access to sandboxes for AI innovators, especially SMEs and startups, by lowering compliance costs and easing entry barriers to the market.

### Testing and Experimentation Facilities (TEFs)

The EU has established specialised Testing and Experimentation Facilities (TEFs) that offer large-scale reference sites where technology providers across Europe can test state-of-the-art AI solutions in real-world environments. These projects will receive over EUR 220 million in combined funding from the European Commission and Member States for a five-year period. Four sector-specific TEFs have been established:

- **Agri-Food:** project "agrifoodTEF"
- **Healthcare:** project "TEF-Health"
- **Manufacturing:** project "AI-MATTERS"
- **Smart Cities and Communities:** project "Citcom.AI"

### European Digital Innovation Hubs (EDIHs)

European Digital Innovation Hubs (EDIHs) are regional one-stop shops that help companies and public sector organisations respond to digital challenges and improve their competitiveness. They offer access to technical expertise and testing (as well as a possibility to "test before invest"), innovation services (such as financial advice), and skills development. There are over 150 hubs operating across the EU.

The 2025 AI Continent Action Plan highlights the role of the EDIH network in facilitating companies' access to regulatory sandboxes. EDIHs will also expand their offering of practical AI training courses tailored to various technical and non-technical backgrounds.

## National Sandbox Initiatives

### Connection to Article 58(3)

Article 58(3) states that prospective providers in the AI regulatory sandboxes, in particular SMEs and start-ups, shall be directed, where relevant, to value-adding services such as Testing and Experimentation Facilities and European Digital Innovation Hubs. This connection is important because regulatory sandboxes are not intended solely for supporting compliance with the AI Act, but also to foster the development and testing of AI systems.

## Belgium

### Overview

In Belgium, there is no national AI regulatory sandbox currently operational, nor has the federal government announced concrete plans to establish one. However, Flanders (Belgium's Dutch-speaking northern region) is actively exploring the possibility through its Department of Economy, Science and Innovation (EWI). In October 2023, the Flemish Government produced a reflection paper examining how an AI Regulatory Sandbox could be implemented within the Belgian governance structure. The proposed sandbox would primarily target SMEs and startups developing high-risk AI applications.

### Key Actors

- **Flemish Department of Economy, Science and Innovation (EWI):** Would coordinate regional implementation
- **Federal Data Protection Authority:** Would provide guidance on privacy compliance
- **Federal market surveillance authorities:** Would contribute sector-specific regulatory expertise
- **Regional innovation agencies (VLAIO, Digital Flanders):** Would provide innovation support and technical expertise
- **Knowledge Centre Data and Society:** Would offer guidance on ethical and societal aspects of AI

### Legal Framework

The reflection paper indicates that a federal-level regulatory framework would be needed for any legal exemptions.

### Operational Structure

**Eligibility criteria:**
- Primary focus on high-risk AI systems as defined in the AI Act
- Priority for SMEs and startups with limited internal regulatory expertise
- Technology Readiness Level (TRL) evaluation, potentially prioritising systems at TRL 4-8

**Proposed tiers of service:**
- **Basic Services:** A central contact point providing risk assessment of AI systems according to the AI Act classification and regulatory guidance
- **Extended Services:** Adding testing environments and tailored individual support paths for companies, potentially leveraging existing innovation infrastructure like Testing and Experimentation Facilities
- **Extended Services with Regulatory Exemptions:** A more ambitious option that would include "safe spaces" with temporary exemptions from certain regulations (noted as legally challenging to implement)

**Testing process:**
1. Application procedure -- companies apply to a central authority that screens based on risk level, technology readiness, and potential impact
2. Test plan development -- selected companies work with experts to develop a customised testing approach
3. Testing phase -- the AI system is examined and tested for regulatory compliance, with guidance on necessary adjustments
4. Exit phase -- successfully evaluated systems receive documentation of their compliance journey

## Czech Republic

### Overview

The Czech Republic is developing a "Digital Regulatory Sandbox" under its National Recovery Plan. While primarily focused on supporting fintech innovation, the scope of the sandbox may also extend to AI products. However, the extent to which the sandbox is relevant to AI systems under the EU AI Act remains unclear.

### Key Actors

- **CzechInvest:** National business development agency responsible for launching and coordinating the sandbox
- **Ministry of Industry and Trade (MPO):** Initiated the sandbox project and oversees its development
- **Ministry of Finance:** Provides input on regulatory design and financial supervision

### Legal Framework

The sandbox's status under the EU AI Act remains unclear. The extent of any legal exemptions for sandbox participants has not been clearly specified.

### Operational Structure

The sandbox will be launched in two phases. The first phase involves the design of the operational framework and finalisation of the support structure. In the second phase, selected high-growth companies, particularly startups and spin-offs, will participate in the sandbox programme. They will receive tailored support, including independent expert advice, legal consultations in emerging regulatory areas, and access to a technical testing environment. The programme has a maximum funding of 150 million Czech crowns (approximately EUR 6 million).

## Denmark

### Overview

Denmark established a regulatory sandbox for AI in early 2024, jointly operated by the Danish Data Protection Authority (Datatilsynet) and the Agency for Digital Government. Initially focused on GDPR compliance, the sandbox plans to expand its scope to include the EU AI Act in the future, supporting both public and private sector AI development. The first sandbox cohort in July 2024 received 23 applications, of which two projects were selected.

### Key Actors

- The Danish Data Protection Authority (Datatilsynet)
- Agency for Digital Government (Digitaliseringsstyrelsen)
- Where relevant, the sandbox may consult other Danish regulators to clarify sector-specific legal issues

### Legal Framework

Initially focused on GDPR compliance, the sandbox does not yet assess compliance with the AI Act, though this is expected to change in the future. The sandbox does not offer legal exemptions. All participants must fully comply with existing data protection legislation.

### Operational Structure

**Eligibility criteria:** The sandbox accepts applications from Denmark-based companies and public authorities with AI projects that process personal data (scope will expand later), offer clear societal benefits, demonstrate innovation, and face identifiable regulatory challenges.

**Testing process:** Participants take part in a structured 6-month programme featuring consultations with regulatory experts. The sandbox concludes with public sharing of insights. However, completing the sandbox process does not mean the product will be approved by Datatilsynet and the Digital Agency.

**Assistance provided:** The sandbox primarily offers legal guidance on navigating data protection requirements, including assistance with data protection impact assessments, implementation of data protection by design principles, and addressing specific regulatory challenges. The programme does not offer technical infrastructure or financial support.

## Estonia

### Overview

Accelerate Estonia is an innovation programme aimed at removing regulatory blocks that prevent innovative products from reaching the market. It provides policymakers concrete policy proposals based on the success of the tested solutions. As of April 2025, Accelerate Estonia has finished 18 projects and has currently 3 projects active. However, it is not clear from the available information whether Accelerate Estonia falls under the mandatory sandbox under the EU AI Act.

### Key Actors

Estonian Ministry of Economic Affairs and Communications. Other ministries are consulted when designing calls for projects.

### Legal Framework

The sandbox is organised under the Estonian Ministry of Economic Affairs and Communications. It has a strong focus on providing policy recommendations to enable innovative solutions to reach the market.

### Operational Structure

**Selection criteria:**
- Address a clearly defined regulatory challenge that requires intervention from the Estonian public sector
- Demonstrate scalability and the potential to solve significant global problems
- Be fully developed and ready for piloting, not at an idea stage
- Be backed by an operational business with a committed, capable team
- Focus on B2B or B2C models (Accelerate Estonia does not support B2G solutions)

**Testing process:** The process begins with identifying ministries open to regulatory innovation, through interviews and public consultations. In collaboration with these ministries, specific problem areas are defined. A public call is then issued for innovative solutions. Chosen participants gain access to government partners, R&D grant opportunities, and the Accelerate Estonia brand. If a solution proves viable, follow-up investments may be offered. Successful projects also serve as the basis for regulatory proposals.

## Finland

### Overview

Finland is in the process of adopting a national legal framework to establish an AI regulatory sandbox in line with the EU AI Act. Additionally, Finland is one of three Member States participating in the pilot phase of the EU Regulatory Sandboxes for AI (EUSAiR) project.

### Key Actors

- **Finnish Transport and Communications Agency (Traficom):** Will coordinate the sandbox
- **Data Protection Ombudsman:** Shares oversight when personal data is processed
- EUSAiR pilot participants include IT Center for Science (CSC), University of Turku, and Haaga-Helia University of Applied Sciences

### Legal Framework

Finland is in the process of adopting legislation for establishing a national AI regulatory sandbox. The proposal takes the form of an amendment to the Act on the Supervision of Certain AI Systems, intended to enter into force on February 1, 2026. The proposal includes provisions on inter-agency coordination, sanctions, and the issuing of compliance-related documentation. It also explicitly enables the secondary use of personal data for testing high-risk AI systems, including in law enforcement contexts. Detailed rules on eligibility, participation procedures, and documentation requirements are deferred to forthcoming EU implementing acts.

### Operational Structure

Applications for participating in the sandbox will be submitted to Traficom, which is required to consult the competent market surveillance authority for the system in question. Many details of the operational structure will be updated based on the upcoming implementing acts.

## France

### Overview

France has established a data protection sandbox programme operated by the national data protection authority (CNIL). While not specifically designed as an AI Act regulatory sandbox, its third edition (launched in 2023) focuses on AI applications for public services.

### Key Actors

- **French data protection authority (CNIL):** Leads and administers the sandbox
- **Evaluation committees:** Composed of three CNIL members and three external experts from relevant public bodies

### Legal Framework

The CNIL sandbox focuses on compliance with GDPR and other data protection regulation. It does not provide exemptions from legal requirements. CNIL specifically notes that "it does not allow the removal of legal constraints, even temporarily, because personal data law does not allow it." Participants must maintain full GDPR compliance.

### Operational Structure

**Eligibility criteria:**
- Public bodies developing AI solutions
- Private organisations partnering with public actors
- Projects addressing needs identified by multiple public entities
- Solutions that are under development but not yet deployed

**Programme phases:**
1. **Support phase (6 months):** Identifying key issues through workshops, training, and collaborative exchanges
2. **Implementation phase:** Participants apply CNIL's recommendations
3. **Knowledge sharing phase:** CNIL summarises findings and recommendations for the broader ecosystem

CNIL offers free expert guidance on navigating data protection requirements for AI systems but provides no technical infrastructure or financial support.

## Germany

### Overview

Germany has institutionalised regulatory sandboxes as a strategic innovation policy tool across various sectors, including AI. This approach began with the "Regulatory Sandboxes Strategy" adopted by the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action (BMWK) in 2018. In November 2024, the Federal Cabinet advanced this work by adopting the draft Regulatory Sandboxes Act (Reallaborgesetz), which provides a comprehensive framework for testing innovations under alternative regulatory conditions.

### Key Actors

- **Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action (BMWK):** Leads the national sandbox policy
- **Network of Regulatory Sandboxes:** Connects stakeholders across sectors

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach is built on "experimentation clauses" within sector-specific legislation that allow authorities to grant temporary exemptions from certain regulatory requirements. The draft Regulatory Sandboxes Act establishes common principles and standards for these exemptions. The draft legislation addresses key challenges, such as streamlining previously inconsistent approval processes, creating opportunities for stakeholder networking, facilitating knowledge transfer to regulatory bodies, and establishing appropriate testing timeframes.

### Operational Structure

The draft Act sets out important mechanisms including the use of experimentation clauses (Paragraph 2), formal processes for regulatory learning and evaluation (Paragraphs 4-7), and regular reporting to Bundestag (Paragraph 9).

A core feature of the law is the **Innovation Portal** ("Reallabore-Innovationsportal"), a platform designed to support the planning and implementation of sandboxes. It will offer legal and organisational guidance, collect information about regulatory barriers, and facilitate connections between government, industry, academia and civil society. The portal is being launched as a pilot that lasts a maximum of four years (Paragraph 3).

## Italy

### Overview

Italy has established a broad regulatory sandbox mechanism called "Sperimentazione Italia" (Experimentation Italy), which provides a framework for testing innovative technologies -- including AI -- under temporary regulatory exemptions. Launched in 2020, it predates the EU AI Act but anticipates its goals. Italy is also one of three Member States selected to host pilot regulatory sandboxes under the EUSAiR initiative.

### Key Actors

- **Department for Digital Transformation** within the Prime Minister's office -- manages the programme
- **Agenzia per l'Italia Digitale (AgID)** -- provides technical support and evaluation during experimentation

### Legal Framework

Sperimentazione Italia was established through Law Decree 76/2020 (the "Digital Simplification" decree), which introduced Article 36 on "Technological Experimentation." This legislation enables temporary exemptions from certain regulations for a limited testing period, while maintaining necessary safety, privacy, and environmental protections. Certain regulatory areas are excluded from possible exemptions, including health protection, environmental protection, cultural and landscape heritage, criminal law provisions, anti-mafia code, EU constraints, and international obligations. Its status under the EU AI Act remains unclear.

### Operational Structure

The process begins with an application submitted to both the Department for Digital Transformation and the Ministry of Economic Development, including a technical annex identifying which regulations require exemption. The programme supports applicants during the application phase, offering preliminary consultations. Following the experimental period, participants must submit a final report. If the experiment is deemed successful, the Department may recommend regulatory changes.

## Luxembourg

### Overview

Luxembourg launched "Sandkescht", a regulatory sandbox in May 2024 operated by the national data protection authority (CNPD). The initiative aims to help innovators develop AI systems compliant with GDPR. Luxembourg's draft law on AI also designates the CNPD as the central coordinator for AI regulation and provides for the establishment of full regulatory sandboxes by August 2026. A separate initiative, the LIST AI Sandbox, is not a regulatory sandbox but rather a technical tool designed to help organisations prepare for the AI Act.

### Key Actors

**The CNPD (Luxembourg data protection authority)** -- operates and oversees the Sandkescht sandbox.

### Legal Framework

Participation in the sandbox does not entail any legal exemption from the GDPR. The sandbox operates within the existing data protection framework while a more comprehensive AI regulatory framework is being developed.

### Operational Structure

**Eligibility criteria:** Both public and private entities may apply, provided they are established in Luxembourg. The project must be centred on new technologies, including AI. Initiatives should tackle data protection issues where regulatory interpretation remains unclear, and must demonstrate clear potential advantages for citizens or broader societal benefits.

**Testing process (three stages):**
1. Participants work with the CNPD to define project goals, develop timelines, and create an exit strategy
2. Implementing the agreed plan with ongoing monitoring
3. Successful practices are formally documented and an exit assessment is completed (9-18 month participation period)

Sandkescht offers primarily legal support rather than technical infrastructure. The CNPD does not provide IT or technical architecture for testing. Technical support is available in the LIST AI Sandbox.

## Malta

### Overview

Malta's national AI strategy addresses the need for regulatory sandboxes. The Member State plans to establish both a "regulatory sandbox" for AI to provide regulatory exemptions for testing new concepts and a "data sandbox" focused specifically on personal data use in AI development.

### Key Actors

- **Malta Digital Innovation Authority (MDIA):** Lead agency for implementing the AI strategy and managing the sandboxes
- **Technology Regulation Advisory Committee:** Works with MDIA to determine the legal and operational structure
- **Office of the Information and Data Protection Commissioner (IDPC):** Acts as the national data protection authority and will set up the "data sandbox"

### Legal Framework

The "regulatory sandbox" aims to provide temporary regulatory exemptions for firms to test AI concepts in a controlled environment. The "data sandbox" will offer guidance on GDPR compliance for AI projects using personal data, with potential assurances against enforcement actions.

### Operational Structure

The sandboxes will be use-case driven. Projects entering the sandbox will be assessed for potential public benefit and monitored closely to manage risks and enhance regulatory learning.

## The Netherlands

### Overview

A proposal for the Dutch implementation of an AI regulatory sandbox has been developed through intensive collaboration between various stakeholders, coordinated by the Ministry of Economic Affairs, the Dutch Data Protection Authority (AP), and the Dutch Authority for Digital Infrastructure (RDI).

### Key Actors

- **Dutch Data Protection Authority (AP)** and **Dutch Authority for Digital Infrastructure (RDI):** Coordinating market surveillance authorities
- Sector-specific market surveillance authorities based on the AI application's domain

### Legal Framework

The Dutch sandbox will focus on providing regulatory guidance without offering exemptions from legal obligations.

### Operational Structure

**Three-pillar structure:**
1. A core team responsible for coordination and answering relatively simple questions
2. A panel of experts for addressing domain-specific questions
3. Sandbox processes involving multiple sessions to collaboratively explore regulatory challenges

**Six phases:**
1. Pre-registration -- information provision and question submission
2. Registration, selection, and first response -- questions are triaged by the Core Team
3. Preparation -- for complex questions, supervisors and applicants develop a sandbox plan
4. Participation in sandbox trajectory -- implementation of the sandbox plan
5. Evaluation and completion -- documentation of results and lessons learned
6. Post-participation -- sharing insights to benefit the broader AI ecosystem

## Slovakia

### Overview

While Slovakia has not yet implemented a national AI regulatory sandbox framework, the Member State has begun exploring AI regulatory sandboxes at a regional level, with the Kosice region serving as a pioneer through its Sandbox AI initiative.

### Key Actors

**Innovation Center of the Kosice Region (ICKK).**

### Legal Framework

The regional sandbox operates without a national legal framework for AI regulatory sandboxes. It functions as a practical testbed initiative rather than a formal regulatory exemption mechanism.

### Operational Structure

The Kosice region's AI Sandbox is focused on AI solutions in public administration. It follows a four-stage process:

1. **Application:** Open to startups, university projects, and established companies with AI solutions that can address public administration challenges
2. **Selection:** Three solutions are selected for the AI Sandbox programme
3. **Implementation and testing:** Selected projects are assigned to relevant departments within the Kosice self-governing region and tested in real practice, supervised by a cross-functional team
4. **Evaluation:** Each project undergoes comprehensive assessment resulting in an output report that evaluates benefits, analyses legal and regulatory implementation possibilities, and examines financial and sustainability models

The best project receives an opportunity to present its solution to Innovation Zurich, offering international exposure.

## Spain

### Overview

Spain established a regulatory sandbox for Artificial Intelligence in 2022 as one of the first European initiatives to test and implement the EU AI Act requirements in practical settings. The sandbox, formalised through Royal Decree 817/2023, creates a controlled test environment for high-risk AI systems and general-purpose AI models. However, practical testing is yet to be started.

### Key Actors

- **The Spanish Agency for the Supervision of Artificial Intelligence (AESIA):** Established in 2023 through Royal Decree 729/2023, oversees AI regulation including sandbox operations
- **The Spanish data protection authority (AEPD):** Retains responsibility for data protection compliance and will collaborate with AESIA

### Legal Framework

Spain enacted Royal Decree 817/2023 establishing a detailed regulatory framework for AI sandboxes. This framework defines eligibility criteria, selection processes, testing requirements, and compliance procedures aligned with the EU AI Act.

### Operational Structure

**Eligibility criteria:**
- Open to both public and private actors established in Spain
- Applications can be submitted by AI system providers or jointly by providers and users
- Systems prohibited under the AI Act or outside its scope (e.g., military purposes) cannot participate
- Further criteria include degree of innovation or technological complexity, social/business/public interest, explainability and transparency, system maturity, type of high-risk AI system (to ensure diversity), quality of technical report, characteristics of the AI provider, and compliance with data protection legislation and Spain's Charter of Digital Rights

**Testing process:** During testing, participants must implement AI Act requirements including risk management systems, data governance frameworks, technical documentation, and human oversight. An implementation plan is agreed between the participant and authorities. Participants collaborate with authorities throughout to ensure compliance. Upon completion of the first stage, participants conduct a self-assessment that authorities review. At the conclusion of testing, participants submit a final report. AESIA then validates it and confirms compliance with the AI Act.

## Sweden

### Overview

Sweden has initiated a pilot into establishing a national AI regulatory sandbox. Two interim reports have been published, the first in June 2024 and the second in December 2024.

Additionally, Sweden launched a regulatory sandbox in 2022, piloted by the Swedish data protection authority (IMY). While the sandbox is generally related to data protection, the initiative announced in 2024 focuses specifically on generative AI systems, testing how generative AI could make it more efficient to handle public documents.

### Key Actors

**Swedish data protection authority (IMY):** Has indicated it expects to have responsibilities in this area and operates the data protection-focused regulatory sandbox.

### Legal Framework

The sandbox operates under the GDPR and Swedish data protection law. Its status under the EU AI Act is unclear. The sandbox does not provide legal exemptions or immunity from enforcement measures. It serves as a regulatory guidance model.

### Operational Structure

IMY's sandbox follows a project-based model:
- IMY selects projects through an internal innovation framework
- Each project receives deep-dive guidance on specific legal questions related to new technologies
- Outcomes are published in public reports to support broader regulatory clarity

The sandbox has completed two projects since 2022, with the third project underway.
