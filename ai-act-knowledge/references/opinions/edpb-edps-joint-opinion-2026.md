# EDPB-EDPS Joint Opinion 1/2026 on the AI Omnibus Proposal

Joint opinion on the European Commission's Digital Omnibus proposal amending the AI Act and other digital legislation.

## Executive Summary

On 19 November 2025, the European Commission issued a Proposal for a Regulation amending Regulations (EU) 2024/1689 and (EU) 2018/1139 as regards the simplification of the implementation of harmonised rules on artificial intelligence (Digital Omnibus on AI). On 25 November 2025, the Commission formally consulted the EDPB and the EDPS in accordance with Article 42(2) of Regulation (EU) 2018/1725.

The EDPB and the EDPS support the Proposal's general objective to address certain implementation challenges of the AI Act, with a view to the effective application of the relevant rules. They recall that the EDPB and the European Commission are working on joint guidelines on the interplay between the GDPR and the AI Act to be issued later in 2026.

### Key Positions

- **Bias detection**: Support in principle extending the legal basis for processing special categories of personal data for bias detection and correction, but recommend maintaining the standard of strict necessity and clearly circumscribing use for non-high-risk AI systems.
- **Registration**: Recommend maintaining the registration obligation in the EU database even where providers conclude a system is not high-risk under Article 6(3) AIA, to preserve accountability and transparency.
- **EU-level sandboxes**: Support creation but recommend DPA involvement, EDPB advisory role, and observer status at the AI Board.
- **AI Office supervision**: Welcome active cooperation requirements but recommend clear delimitation of exclusive competence and close coordination with DPAs.
- **Fundamental rights authorities**: Support streamlined FRAB-MSA cooperation but recommend clarifying MSA role as administrative contact point only.
- **AI literacy**: Recommend maintaining the obligation on providers and deployers rather than replacing it with a softer "encourage" approach.
- **Timeline**: Express concern about the proposed postponement of high-risk rules but acknowledge partially objective reasons for delay.

## Section 1: Background

### Point 1

On 19 November 2025, the European Commission issued the Proposal for a Regulation amending Regulations (EU) 2024/1689 and (EU) 2018/1139 as regards the simplification of the implementation of harmonised rules on artificial intelligence (Digital Omnibus on AI). On 25 November 2025, the Commission formally consulted the EDPB and the EDPS in accordance with Article 42(2) of Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 (EUDPR).

### Point 2

The Proposal aims to amend Regulation 2024/1689 (the AI Act) to address certain implementation challenges by way of targeted simplification measures. The proposed amendments relate to various topics, ranging from the implementation timeline of the rules for high-risk AI systems, to reducing the registration burden for certain AI systems and extending regulatory simplifications granted to SMEs to small mid-caps (SMCs).

### Point 3

The aim of this Joint Opinion is not to provide an assessment of all the proposed amendments, but instead, to address the most relevant aspects of the Proposal which are of particular importance for the protection of individuals' rights and freedoms with regard to the processing of personal data.

---

## Section 2: General Remarks

### Point 4

The EDPB and the EDPS support the Proposal's general objective to address certain implementation challenges of the AI Act. They recall that the EDPB and the European Commission are working on joint guidelines on the interplay between the GDPR and the AI Act to be issued later in 2026. This echoes the commitments of the EDPB in its Helsinki Statement to take up initiatives to facilitate GDPR compliance and strengthen consistency, in order to empower responsible innovation and reinforce competitiveness in Europe.

### Point 5

Another aim of the Proposal is to significantly reduce the administrative burden for businesses, national administrations, and the public at large. The EDPB and the EDPS support this general objective, as long as pursuing this objective does not result in lowering the protection of fundamental rights of individuals, in particular the fundamental right to protection of personal data. They also recall that, already during the initial drafting of the AI Act, several amendments were introduced to help reduce administrative burdens while still protecting the fundamental rights of individuals. A careful balance needs to be kept between reducing administrative burden where possible, without undermining the protection of fundamental rights in the context of AI. The EDPB and the EDPS warn against reducing the existing protection offered under the AI Act without careful consideration of the protection of the rights of individuals.

### Point 6

The references to Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) in this Joint Opinion should be understood as the supervisory authorities within the meaning of Article 4(21) GDPR and Article 3(15) Law Enforcement Directive (LED), regardless of whether they have been entrusted with additional tasks or powers under the AI Act, as market surveillance authorities (MSAs).

## Section 3: Processing of Special Categories of Personal Data for Bias Detection and Correction

### Point 7

According to Article 10(5) AI Act, providers of high-risk systems may exceptionally process special categories of personal data to the extent that it is strictly necessary for the purpose of ensuring bias detection and correction in accordance with the requirements of Article 10(2)(f) and (g) AI Act. Such processing must be subject to appropriate safeguards for the fundamental rights and freedoms of natural persons.

### Point 8

The Proposal would introduce a new Article 4a, replacing Article 10(5), that would allow, where necessary, processing of special categories of personal data by providers and deployers of all AI systems and models, subject to appropriate safeguards that complement the GDPR, EUDPR and LED. The Proposal would extend the material and personal scope to all AI systems and models and would also cover deployers.

### Point 9

While the processing of special categories of personal data for bias detection and correction can entail additional risks to the fundamental rights and freedoms of the affected data subjects, it is also true that if bias detection and correction are unsuccessful or insufficient, bias in AI systems may present a wider risk to those whose personal data would be processed by the AI system after it has been put on the market, and even to society as a whole.

### Point 10

The EDPB and the EDPS also understand that the list of high-risk AI systems can never be exhaustive. Consequently, systems that have not been identified as high-risk could still lead, in certain cases, to negative consequences for individuals.

### Point 11

The EDPB and the EDPS recall that processing special categories of personal data is in principle prohibited under EU data protection law and the exceptions to this prohibition should be narrowly defined. To avoid potential abuse, the cases where providers and deployers would be able to rely on this legal ground in the context of non-high risk AI systems and models should be clearly circumscribed and limited to cases where the risk of adverse effects caused by such bias is sufficiently serious to justify the processing of special categories of personal data.

### Point 12: Recommendation on Strict Necessity Standard

The current text of Article 10(5) AI Act refers to processing that is "strictly necessary." The proposed new Article 4a(1) refers only to "necessary," while Article 4a(2) refers to "necessary and proportionate." The EDPB and the EDPS recommend reinstating the standard of strict necessity, which currently applies for high-risk AI systems, for all providers and deployers of AI systems and models referred to in Article 4a.

### Point 13

Current Article 10(5) AI Act limits the possibility to process special categories of personal data to cases where it is necessary to comply with the obligations under Article 10(2)(f) and (g) AI Act. The EDPB and the EDPS stress that the scope of the new Article 4a(2) should be understood as similarly limited to the detection, prevention and mitigation of biases that are likely to affect the health and safety of persons, have a negative impact on fundamental rights or lead to discrimination prohibited under Union law.

### Point 14: Recommendation on Detailed Justification

The EDPB and the EDPS recommend providing, by way of a recital, more detailed justifications for the envisaged extension of the scope of the exception by indicating specific examples of non-high-risk AI systems or models that could adversely affect individuals based on protected characteristics.

### Point 15: Recommendation on Legal Certainty

The wording of Article 4a(2) should be revised to enhance legal certainty as regards the application of Articles 6 and 9 GDPR. The use of "may" is likely to give rise to legal uncertainty. If it is the intention to extend the duty under current Article 10(2)(f) and (g) AI Act to providers and deployers of all AI systems and models, this should be clearly stated. The EDPB and the EDPS recommend replacing "paragraph 1 may apply" with "paragraph 1 shall also apply" and replacing "if the processing occurs" with "where such processing occurs."

### Point 16: DPA Competence

The EDPB and the EDPS recall that the current provision of Article 10(5) AI Act and thus the new Article 4a regulate a specific case of processing of special categories of personal data as a matter of substantial public interest within the meaning of Article 9(2)(g) GDPR and Article 10(2)(g) EUDPR. All the conditions laid down in the GDPR and the EUDPR would fully apply. The Commission explicitly clarifies that the aim of the amendment is "facilitating compliance with the data protection laws." Against this background, the EDPB and the EDPS stress that DPAs would first and foremost be competent to supervise the processing of personal data pursuant to Article 4a AIA, in line with Article 2(7) AIA.

## Section 4: Registration and Documentation

### Point 17: Recommendation to Maintain Registration Obligation

The EDPB and the EDPS support the general aim of the Proposal to ease administrative burdens for operators. However, they recommend maintaining the obligation for providers to register AI systems in the EU database for high-risk systems also in the cases where the provider has concluded the system is -- despite being referred to in Annex III AI Act -- not high-risk, under the conditions set by Article 6(3) AIA.

### Point 18

The registration obligation ensures the transparency and traceability of these systems towards the public as well as the national competent authorities. Given that the AI systems in question could still pose potentially significant risks, the registration obligation does not seem unreasonable or disproportionate. The proposed deletion of the existing obligation would significantly decrease the accountability of providers of AI systems and would provide an undesirable incentive for providers to unduly invoke this exemption without critical analysis.

### Point 19: Public Transparency

The registration obligation allows the public to be informed about the grounds under which the provider considers that the AI system does not pose a significant risk. The public registration further allows deployers to carry out proper due diligence and risk-management. The public disclosure and the related possible reputational risks for providers are better aligned with the possible risks posed by these AI systems.

### Point 20: Informing Authorities

Registration serves the purpose of informing national competent authorities and FRABs before these systems are placed in the market or put into service, which may lead them to requesting documentation and engaging in enforcement activities. The leeway afforded to providers does not appear to be justified by the negligible savings (maximum of 1,485 companies saving EUR 100 each, for total savings up to EUR 148,500 per year).

### Point 21

While providers would still be obliged to document their assessment and make it available upon request, this does not appear to be sufficient if not accompanied by the registration. The exemption foreseen by Article 6(3) and 6(4) AIA must remain counter-balanced by appropriate accountability, also considering the existing differing interpretations and risks of incorrect assessment.

### Point 22: SME/SMC Extensions

The EDPB and the EDPS note that the Proposal extends certain regulatory privileges to SMEs and SMCs, defined on the basis of staff headcount and turnover or balance sheet numbers.

### Point 23

In light of AI's features of scalability and autonomy, the headcount and more generally the company size may not be a decisive factor to assess the possible harm posed by high-risk AI systems placed on the market by such enterprises.

### Point 24: Concern on Size-Based Simplification

The EDPB and the EDPS express concerns as to the approach of simplifying the obligations relating to product safety of AI systems on the basis of the size of the company and especially headcount.

## Section 5: AI Regulatory Sandboxes at Union Level

### Point 25

The EDPB and the EDPS welcome the introduction of AI regulatory sandboxes at EU level to support innovation and SMEs across the EEA (new Article 57(3a) AIA). The proposed changes introduce the possibility for the AI Office to establish such sandboxes with regard to certain AI systems, such as those based on a general-purpose AI model. These sandboxes would be in addition to those at national level.

### Point 26: Recommendation on DPA Involvement

Article 57(10) AIA currently requires that national DPAs are associated with national sandboxes. There is no similar provision for EU-level sandboxes, even though they may involve processing of personal data subject to DPA oversight. The EDPB and the EDPS recommend clarifying, directly in the AI Act, that competent DPAs should be associated with the operation of EU-level sandboxes and involved in the supervision and enforcement of the corresponding data processing in line with Articles 55 et seq. GDPR.

### Point 27: Recommendation on DPA Competence Clarity

It is unclear how the competent DPA would be identified in the context of EU-level sandboxes and how this relates to the GDPR cooperation mechanism. Since association to a sandbox involves significant limitations to DPA powers (as per Article 57(12) AI Act), the issue of DPA competence and its interplay with the GDPR cooperation mechanism should be addressed directly in the AI Act.

### Point 28: Recommendation on EDPB Advisory Role

The EDPB should be involved to ensure a coordinated and consistent approach. The EDPB and the EDPS recommend modifying Article 57 AIA to specifically refer to the advisory role of the EDPB for ensuring consistency on data protection aspects, specifically in cases where several DPAs would be concerned by the AI system developed in the EU-level sandbox.

### Point 29: Recommendation on EDPB Observer Status

The EDPB should be granted, directly in the AI Act, the status of observer at the European Artificial Intelligence Board (AI Board). This could ensure the continuous involvement of the EDPB when matters related to data protection law are discussed within the AI Board.

### Point 30

The new obligation for national competent authorities to support the joint establishment and operation of AI regulatory sandboxes (revised Article 57(14) AIA) is welcomed. However, how such obligation will be operationalised in practice remains unclear, and the EDPB and the EDPS recommend clarifying these aspects.

### Point 31: Distinction Between EU-Level Sandboxes

A clear distinction should be made between AI sandboxes for Union institutions, bodies, offices or agencies (established by the EDPS pursuant to Article 57(3) AIA) and the EU-level AI sandbox established by the AI Office pursuant to Article 57(3a) AIA. AI systems placed on the market, put into service or used by Union institutions would not be involved in the AI regulatory sandbox at EU-level, as the EDPS is the sole competent authority for such systems. The EDPS remains the competent authority under Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 to supervise any processing of personal data by the AI Office itself in the context of EU-level sandboxes.

## Section 6: Supervision and Enforcement by the AI Office

### Point 32

Pursuant to Article 75 AI Act, the AI Office is competent to monitor and supervise compliance of AI systems based on a general-purpose AI model when the model and the system are developed by the same provider. The Proposal further extends this exclusive competence to AI systems that constitute or are integrated into a designated very large online platform (VLOP) or very large online search engine (VLOSE) within the meaning of the Digital Services Act.

### Point 33: Recommendations on AI Office Supervision

The EDPB and the EDPS acknowledge that monitoring, supervision, and enforcement of such systems would benefit from centralising competencies at the EU level. They welcome the active cooperation requirement in Article 75(1), last subparagraph AIA. However, this may not be sufficient to guarantee the ability of national competent authorities to initiate actions if the AI Office has not already acted or does not want to, given the exclusivity of the authority granted to the AI Office. The AI Office should coordinate closely with the competent national data protection authorities when the AI systems present risks to the fundamental rights to privacy and data protection, in compliance with Article 8(3) of the Charter of Fundamental Rights and in line with Article 2(7) AIA. The EDPB and the EDPS recommend clearly delimiting the types of general-purpose AI models that trigger "exclusive competence" to ensure effective supervision.

### Point 34: Recommendation on EDPS Supervision Exclusion

Recital 14 of the Proposal clarifies that the AI Office would not be competent for AI systems placed on the market, put into service or used by Union institutions, bodies, offices or agencies, which are under the supervision of the EDPS pursuant to Article 74(9) AI Act. However, this clarification is not provided in the operative part of the Regulation. The EDPB and the EDPS consider it necessary that the amended Article 75 should expressly exclude from the competence of the AI Office the supervision of AI systems developed or used by Union institutions covered by Article 74(9) AIA.

## Section 7: Powers of Authorities/Bodies Protecting Fundamental Rights and Cooperation with MSA

### Point 35

The EDPB and the EDPS support clarifying the scope of cooperation between MSAs and FRABs. This is in line with the EDPB's recommendations in Statement 3/2024 on data protection authorities' role in the AIA.

### Point 36: Recommendation on Centralised Contact Point

Article 77 AIA currently allows FRABs to directly request documentation from deployers or providers. The proposed change would place MSAs as an intermediary. The establishment of a centralised point of contact is welcomed, but requiring FRABs to obtain information solely through MSAs may actually result in inefficiencies. The EDPB and the EDPS recommend ensuring the centralised contact point achieves, in practice, the objective of making the procedure more efficient.

### Point 37: Recommendation on MSA Role Clarification

The EDPB and the EDPS recommend explicitly clarifying that the role of MSAs should be strictly that of an administrative point of contact for the execution and transmission of requests, and should not lead to MSAs assessing the necessity or proportionality of the request.

### Point 38: Recommendation on DPA Independence

The Proposal should clarify that it does not affect the independence and existing powers of DPAs, in particular the powers of DPAs to obtain, from the controller and the processor, access to all information necessary for monitoring compliance with data protection law (Article 58(1)(e) GDPR and Article 47(1) LED).

### Point 39: Recommendation on Cross-Border Cooperation

Article 77(1a) AIA should be more specific to build a workable information exchange system in case of cross-border cooperation. The Proposal should specify that MSAs should provide the information requested by FRABs without undue delay, both at national level and in cross-border cases.

### Point 40

Should the proposed changes to Article 6(4) AIA (registration obligation) be maintained, the clarifications on FRAB-MSA cooperation would be all the more beneficial as the changes would reduce publicly available information.

### Point 41: Recommendation on Mutual Assistance

The EDPB and the EDPS positively note the addition of Article 77(1b) AIA creating a new obligation of cooperation and mutual assistance between MSAs and FRABs. This could be beneficial for cross-border cooperation, provided it aligns with the principle of sincere cooperation under Article 4(3) TEU. The new obligation could be further completed by clarifying how mutual assistance under Article 77(1b) AIA interplays with cross-border mutual assistance for market surveillance under Regulation (EU) 2019/1020 and Article 75 AIA.

## Section 8: AI Literacy

### Point 42: Recommendation to Maintain AI Literacy Obligation

The EDPB and the EDPS recommend maintaining the obligation, for providers and deployers of AI systems, to take measures to ensure a sufficient level of AI literacy of their staff and other relevant persons (Article 4 AIA).

### Point 43

AI literacy ensures an understanding of AI concepts and helps raise ethical and social awareness about the benefits and risks of AI. It plays a key role in empowering individuals that develop, deploy, or otherwise work with AI systems and allows them to develop critical thinking with regard to this new technology and inform their decision-making. AI literacy is crucial to protect fundamental rights, including the right to data protection, and support compliance with AI rules, including the provisions on processing of personal data.

### Point 44

Transforming the current obligation into an obligation for the European Commission and the Member States to "encourage" providers and deployers to do so would significantly soften this obligation and ultimately undermine its very objective.

### Point 45

To help providers and deployers comply with their AI-literacy obligation, the European Commission and regulators could be required to issue guidance on how this obligation may be implemented in practice, instead of removing the AI literacy requirement on providers and deployers.

### Point 46

If the new obligation for the European Commission and Member States to "encourage" providers and deployers would be kept, it should apply in parallel with the current obligation in Article 4 AI Act, as opposed to replacing it.

## Section 9: Implementation Timeline of High-Risk Rules

### Point 47

Under the current Article 113 AI Act, the provisions governing high-risk AI systems included in Annex III will start to apply from 2 August 2026. For high-risk AI systems under Annex I, the starting date is 2 August 2027.

### Point 48

Pursuant to the Proposal, the rules on high-risk AI systems should start to apply 6 or 12 months following the Commission's decision confirming that adequate compliance support measures are available, but not later than 2 December 2027 for Annex III systems and 2 August 2028 for Annex I systems. The Commission justifies the proposed delay with implementation challenges such as delays in designating national competent authorities and conformity assessment bodies, as well as a lack of harmonised standards, guidance, and compliance tools.

### Point 49: Grandfathering Clause Extension

The proposed postponement should also be considered in the context of the extension of the "grandfathering clause" of Article 111(2) AI Act, pursuant to which high-risk AI systems already placed in the EU market would largely be excluded from the scope of the Act unless they are subject to significant changes in their design. The cut-off date would be changed from 2 August 2026 to 2 December 2027, allowing an increased number of high-risk AI systems to benefit from the exception as "legacy" systems.

### Point 50: Concern on Fundamental Rights Impact

While the EDPB and the EDPS acknowledge that some of the reasons for the delay may be deemed at least partially objective and not fully under the Commission's control, they are nevertheless sincerely concerned about the potential impact on the protection of fundamental rights in the fast-evolving and transformative AI landscape. It should be assessed whether the introduction of a moveable deadline would also undermine legal certainty. The EDPB and the EDPS recall that, in their Joint Opinion 5/2021 on the AI Act proposal, they had already objected to the exemption from obligations for AI systems already placed on the market.

### Point 51: Recommendation to Preserve Certain Timelines

Given the different nature of the obligations for providers and deployers of high-risk AI systems laid down in Chapter III, Sections 1, 2, and 3 AI Act, the EDPB and the EDPS invite the co-legislators to consider whether it would be appropriate and feasible to maintain the current timeline for certain obligations, e.g. on transparency.

### Point 52

If the proposed delay of the timeline is nevertheless adopted, the EDPB and the EDPS call for concerted actions by all relevant stakeholders, and in particular by the Commission, in order to minimise the delay to the extent possible.
