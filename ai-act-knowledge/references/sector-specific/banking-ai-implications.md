## Introduction to the AI Act

In August 2024, the EU's AI Act (Regulation EU 2024/1689) entered into force. Its main goals include creating a single set of rules for how AI systems work across the EU and promoting AI that is safe, trustworthy, and protective of fundamental rights.

The AI Act defines AI systems as machine-based systems that can work with autonomy and may learn or adapt after being used. These systems take input data and use it to produce outcomes (predictions, content, recommendations, decisions) that can influence the physical or digital environments.

---

## High-Risk AI Systems in Banking

Based on the significance of each AI system's risks to fundamental rights, some systems are classified as "high-risk." In the banking and payment sector, the use of AI systems to evaluate the creditworthiness or establish the credit score of natural persons is classified as "high-risk," and the AI Act introduces additional safeguards for these systems.

### Provider vs. Deployer Obligations

The scope of requirements applying to financial institutions depends, among others, on whether they:

- **Develop AI systems in-house** — in which case they are both providers and deployers of the AI system
- **Use AI systems developed by a third-party provider** — in which case the financial institution is only a deployer

The European Commission is mandated to issue Guidelines on the classification of high-risk use cases by 2 February 2026, which is expected to provide further guidance and practical examples of high-risk use cases.

## The EBA's AI Act Mapping Exercise

In 2025, the EBA mapped the AI Act requirements on high-risk AI systems, with a focus on the use of AI for creditworthiness and credit scoring, against the sectoral requirements included in the Acts within the EBA's scope of action. The mapped legislation includes the Capital Requirements Directive (CRD), Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR), the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), Consumer Credit Directive (CCD), Mortgage Credit Directive (MCD), Payment Services Directive (PSD), and EBA Guidelines (e.g., on Loan Origination and Monitoring).

The purpose of the mapping exercise was to assess and promote a common understanding of the potential regulatory and supervisory implications of the AI Act for the EU banking and payments sectors.

---

## Key Findings

### No Significant Contradictions

No significant contradictions have been found between the AI Act and EU banking and payment legislation.

### Complementarity with Existing Framework

The AI Act is complementary to EU banking and payment sector legislation, which already provides a comprehensive framework to manage risks. Some efforts may be required by banks and other financial institutions to integrate the two frameworks effectively.

### Balancing Objectives

Applying the AI Act may involve balancing the goals of protection of fundamental rights, health, and safety with the objectives of other sectoral laws.

### Supervisory Cooperation

The co-existence of multiple authorities (prudential/conduct authorities and Market Surveillance Authorities) supervising financial entities' compliance highlights the importance of supervisory cooperation to ensure effective implementation of the AI Act.

---

## Alignment Assessment

The EBA's mapping produced a visual summary categorising the interplay between AI Act requirements on high-risk AI and mapped EU financial services legislation into four levels:

- **Fully aligned:** The AI Act obligation is substantively identical to requirements under EU financial services legislation.
- **Partially aligned:** The AI Act obligation and the financial services provision share common elements, but there are differences in scope, terminology, or implementation requirements.
- **Complementary:** The AI Act obligation addresses a comparable issue but extends or adds a new dimension/objective to financial services law requirements.
- **Not present in Financial Services Law:** The AI Act obligation is not addressed by a comparable provision under EU financial services legislation.

---

## Regulatory Synergies and Derogations

To avoid introducing unnecessary and unintended regulatory duplications, the AI Act acknowledges that some requirements may overlap with EU sectoral legislation. The Act therefore sets out targeted derogations and synergies between the AI Act and EU financial services law.

Even where the AI Act does not envisage derogations, the EBA found that EU banking and payments sector legislation already includes a wide range of requirements for many of the relevant aspects. The EBA has shared these findings with the AI Office as input to the European Commission's forthcoming Guidelines on the interplay between the AI Act and EU sectoral law.

---

## EBA Conclusions

The EBA has not identified any immediate need to introduce new or review existing EBA Guidelines. Instead, the EBA will follow up with actions to contribute to a common supervisory approach for cooperation and implementation of sectoral requirements alongside AI Act requirements.

---

## Next Steps

In 2026/2027, the EBA will undertake specific activities to support the implementation of the AI Act in the EU banking and payments sector by:

- Promoting a common supervisory approach and supervisory cooperation among national competent authorities in charge of financial sector supervision and market surveillance authorities
- Providing input to the AI Office, as appropriate, and participating in discussions of the AI Board Subgroup on Financial Services

### Limitations

The EBA's mapping exercise must be considered in light of limitations from future European Commission guidelines and European harmonised standards. It does not represent guidance or supervisory expectations, nor an official/legal position or advice, and may be subject to further clarifications as AI Office and AI Board guidance evolve.
