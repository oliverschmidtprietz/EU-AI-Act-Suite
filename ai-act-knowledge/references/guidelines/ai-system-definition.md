# EC Guidelines on AI System Definition

Commission guidelines to facilitate the first steps of implementation — clarifying the definition of an AI system under Art. 3(1) of the AI Act.

## Purpose and scope

By issuing guidelines on the AI system definition, the Commission aims to assist providers and other relevant persons in determining whether a software system constitutes an AI system to facilitate the effective application of the rules.

The guidelines on the AI system definition are not binding. They are designed to evolve over time and will be updated as necessary, in particular in light of practical experiences, new questions and use cases that arise.

## Context

The AI Act, which aims to promote innovation while ensuring high levels of health, safety, and fundamental rights protection, classifies AI systems into different risk categories, including prohibited, high-risk, and those subject to transparency obligations.

As of 2 February 2025, the first rules under the [Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act)](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai) have started to apply. These include the AI system definition, AI literacy, and a very limited number of prohibited AI use cases that pose unacceptable risks in the EU, as outlined in the AI Act.

The Commission has published these guidelines in addition to the [Guidelines on prohibited artificial intelligence (AI) practices](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/commission-publishes-guidelines-prohibited-artificial-intelligence-ai-practices-defined-ai-act), as defined by the AI Act.

## The definition explained

The guidelines on the AI system definition explain the practical application of the legal concept, as anchored in the AI Act.

### Art. 3(1) — AI system definition

Under Art. 3(1), an **AI system** is defined as:

> a machine-based system that is designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy and that may exhibit adaptiveness after deployment, and that, for explicit or implicit objectives, infers, from the input it receives, how to generate outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions that can influence physical or virtual environments.

### Key elements of the definition

The definition contains several cumulative criteria that must all be met for a system to qualify as an AI system:

1. **Machine-based system** — The system must be a machine-based (i.e. software and/or hardware) system, distinguishing it from purely human processes.

2. **Designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy** — The system must be capable of operating with some degree of autonomy, meaning it can function with some level of independence from human control.

3. **May exhibit adaptiveness after deployment** — The system may (but does not have to) continue to learn and adapt its behaviour after it has been deployed.

4. **For explicit or implicit objectives** — The system must be designed to pursue objectives, whether these are explicitly programmed or implicitly derived from its training.

5. **Infers from input** — The system must use inference processes to derive outputs from the inputs it receives. This is a core distinguishing feature separating AI systems from simpler software.

6. **Generates outputs** — The system produces outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions.

7. **Can influence physical or virtual environments** — The outputs must be capable of influencing environments, whether physical or virtual.

### Boundary cases and exclusions

The guidelines clarify that not all software qualifies as an AI system. Systems that operate on the basis of rules defined solely by natural persons to automatically execute operations (traditional software) are generally not considered AI systems. The key distinguishing factor is the **inference** capability — the system's ability to derive outputs that go beyond simple data processing or rule execution.

Recital 12 of the AI Act further specifies that the definition should not cover systems based on rules defined solely by natural persons to automatically execute operations, such as:

- Simple rule-based systems / classical automation
- Basic statistical methods
- Database management systems
- Conventional search engines
