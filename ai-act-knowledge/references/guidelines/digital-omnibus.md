## Overview

The European Commission published COM(2025) 836 final on 19 November 2025, proposing targeted amendments to the AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) and to the aviation safety regulation (Regulation (EU) 2018/1139). The aim is to ensure **timely, smooth, and proportionate implementation** of the AI Act's provisions without reducing fundamental rights protections.

The proposal follows extensive consultation: three spring-2025 public consultations (718 responses), a September-October 2025 call for evidence (513 responses), an SME panel survey (106 responses), and bilateral Member State discussions in Council working parties.

Estimated cost savings: **EUR 297.2-433.2 million**. Key beneficiaries include SMEs, small mid-caps (SMCs), startups, and Member States.

The legal instrument is a Regulation adopted via ordinary legislative procedure (Article 114 TFEU), to enter into force as a matter of urgency.

Full proposal text: [CELEX:52025PC0836](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:52025PC0836)

## Proposed Simplifications

### AI Literacy Obligation Restructured (Article 4)

The obligation shifts from a direct mandate on providers and deployers to train their staff to a **Commission/Member State promotion duty**. The new language "encourages providers and deployers... to take measures to ensure sufficient level of AI literacy," accounting for technical knowledge, experience, and use context.

### Bias Detection and Data Processing (New Article 4a)

A new provision permits processing of **special category personal data** for bias detection and correction across all AI systems (not limited to high-risk), subject to safeguards:

- Processing necessity must be documented
- Technical limitations on data re-use
- State-of-the-art security and pseudonymization
- Strict access controls and deletion timelines
- Alignment with GDPR (Regulation 2016/679), Regulation 2018/1725, and Directive 2016/680

### Registration Burden Reduction (Article 49)

Eliminates the Article 49(2) requirement for EU database registration of AI systems assessed as non-high-risk under Article 6(3). Providers must still document assessment justifications before market placement.

### Post-Market Monitoring Flexibility (Article 72)

Removes the Article 72(3) empowerment for a harmonized monitoring plan template. Instead, providers design plans tailored to their organisations, and the Commission publishes guidance.

### Code of Practice Flexibility (Articles 50, 56)

Removes empowerments for Commission implementing acts giving codes of practice "general validity." The AI Office and Board encourage Union-level codes; the Commission assesses adequacy. This shifts from mandatory harmonization to a **voluntary framework with Commission oversight**.

### Scientific Panel Access (Article 69)

Member States may directly consult panel experts without Commission involvement. Expert remuneration is equivalent to the Commission's obligations in similar circumstances.

## Changes to High-Risk Classification

### Conformity Assessment Streamlining

**Single application procedure (Article 28(8)):** Conformity assessment bodies applying for designation under both the AI Act and Union harmonization legislation (Section A, Annex I -- covering medical devices, in-vitro diagnostics, etc.) submit a single application, avoiding duplications.

**Notified body scope definition (Article 30):** A new Annex XIV establishes codes, categories, and types for AI systems via a three-dimensional typology:

- **AIP codes** -- product-legislation-linked AI
- **AIB codes** -- biometric AI
- **AIH codes** -- horizontal AI technology types

The Commission gains delegated act power to amend this typology in line with technical progress.

**Product legislation integration (Article 43(3)):** Systems under Section A Annex I follow the product legislation's conformity assessment procedure; AI Act Section 2 requirements are integrated into that assessment. Notified bodies under product law may assess AI compliance if they meet the requirements of Article 31(4), (5), (10), (11). If a system falls under both Section A and Annex III, the product legislation's procedure governs.

### Real-World Testing Expansion (Article 60)

- Real-world testing extended beyond AI regulatory sandboxes to high-risk systems under Section A Annex I
- Testing plans integrated into a single sandbox plan document when applicable
- Testing allowed "at any time before placing on market or putting into service"

**New Article 60a:** Voluntary agreements between Commission and Member States enable testing of Section B Annex I systems (product-legislation-integrated high-risk), bypassing the sandbox requirement.

## SME and Small Mid-Cap Relief Measures

The proposal extends existing SME privileges to **small mid-caps (SMCs)** -- enterprises exceeding micro/small/medium definitions. New formal definitions reference Commission Recommendation 2003/361/EC and Recommendation 2025/1099.

Specific relief measures:

- **Simplified technical documentation** requirements (Annex IV simplified form) for SMEs and SMCs
- **Quality management system** compliance proportionality extended to SMCs
- **Penalty provisions** adjusted for SMCs, mirroring existing SME treatment
- **Guidance availability** expanded to support SMC compliance
- **Priority access** to AI regulatory sandboxes for SMEs

## AI Regulatory Sandboxes

### Union-Level Sandbox (Article 57(3a))

The AI Office establishes an EU-level sandbox for Article 75(1) systems, operating in cooperation with relevant competent authorities, with priority access for SMEs.

### Operational Improvements (Article 57(5), (13), (14))

- Sandbox plans incorporate real-world testing in a single document
- Cross-border cooperation facilitated by design
- National authorities coordinate through the Board framework
- Joint establishment supported across sectors

### Implementation Framework (Article 58)

Commission adopts implementing acts specifying eligibility/selection criteria, application/participation/exit procedures, sandbox plans and exit reports, and governance terms and coordination rules.

## Timeline and Transitional Provisions

### Flexible Entry into Application for High-Risk Rules (Article 113)

A new mechanism links entry into application of Chapter III (high-risk obligations) to **availability of supporting measures**. The Commission must confirm availability of:

- Harmonized standards
- Common specifications
- Commission guidelines

Application schedule after confirmation:

| System Category | Transition Period | Hard Deadline |
|-----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Annex III systems (e.g. biometric) | 6 months after confirmation | 2 December 2027 |
| Annex I systems (product-linked) | 12 months after confirmation | 2 August 2028 |

Without confirmation, rules apply by the hard deadlines regardless. This replaces the general 2 August 2026 application date for high-risk obligations.

### Generative AI Marking Transitional Period (Article 111(30))

A 6-month transitional period after 2 August 2026 allows providers that placed systems before that date to retroactively implement machine-readable marking for detection of artificially generated or manipulated content.

### Grace Period Clarification (Article 111(2))

The grace period applies to an AI system "type and model." If one unit was lawfully placed before the application date, other units of the identical type and model may continue to be placed without new obligations or certification, provided the design is unchanged. Significant design modifications trigger full compliance requirements.

### Conformity Assessment Body Transition

Notified bodies under product legislation (Section A) must apply for AI Act designation within 18 months. A single assessment procedure builds on existing product-law designations.

## AI Office Supervision Expansion (Article 75)

### Expanded Scope

The AI Office supervises **general-purpose AI (GPAI) model-based systems** where the model and system come from the same provider. It also brings GPAI systems embedded in **very large online platforms (VLOPs)** or **very large online search engines (VLOSEs)** (per Regulation 2022/2065) under AI Office supervision.

Exclusions:

- Systems related to Section A Annex I products (sectoral authorities retain responsibility)
- AI systems placed or used by EU institutions (European Data Protection Supervisor retains supervision)

### Enforcement Powers

The Commission may specify AI Office enforcement powers via implementing acts, drawing from Regulation 2019/1020 Article 14. Powers include imposing fines and administrative sanctions per Article 99 conditions and ceilings. Procedural rights per Article 18 of that regulation apply to providers. The Commission is also authorized to conduct pre-market conformity assessments for third-party-assessed systems.

### Coordination with Digital Services Act

Information sharing with authorities enforcing the Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065), including mutual cooperation on risk assessments, mitigating measures, audits, regular exchange on enforcement actions, and consideration of prior fines/penalties for the same conduct to ensure proportionality (non bis in idem principle).

## Fundamental Rights Cooperation (Article 77)

Clarifies the scope of cooperation for authorities and bodies enforcing fundamental-rights-protective Union law. These authorities may request access to information and documentation via competent market surveillance authorities, with mutual cooperation obligations established.

## Aviation Regulation Amendment

Article 2 makes a technical correction to Regulation (EU) 2018/1139 Article 108, extending specific articles to ensure AI Act high-risk requirements are fully covered in delegated and implementing acts without interfering with aviation governance, conformity assessment, or enforcement.
