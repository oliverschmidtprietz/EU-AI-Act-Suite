## 1. Background and Objectives

(1) Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (the AI Act) entered into force on 1 August 2024. It lays down harmonised rules for the placing on the market, putting into service, and use of AI in the Union, aiming to promote innovation while ensuring a high level of protection of health, safety and fundamental rights, including democracy and the rule of law.

(2) The AI Act follows a risk-based approach with four risk categories:
- **Unacceptable risk**: Prohibited under Article 5 AI Act
- **High risk**: Subject to requirements and obligations (Article 6 AI Act, Annexes I and III)
- **Transparency risk**: Subject to transparency obligations (Article 50 AI Act)
- **Minimal to no risk**: Not regulated; voluntary codes of conduct (Article 95 AI Act)

(3) Pursuant to Article 96(1)(b) AI Act, the Commission is to adopt guidelines on the practical implementation of the practices prohibited under Article 5. Those prohibitions apply as from 2 February 2025.

(4) These Guidelines aim to increase legal clarity and provide insights into the Commission's interpretation of the prohibitions. They should serve as practical guidance for competent authorities, providers and deployers of AI systems.

(5) These Guidelines are non-binding. Authoritative interpretation may ultimately only be given by the CJEU.

(7) Application of Article 5 requires a case-by-case assessment. The examples are merely indicative.

## 2. Overview of Prohibited AI Practices

(8) Article 5 AI Act prohibits the placing on the EU market, putting into service, or use of certain AI systems for manipulative, exploitative, social control or surveillance practices, which by their inherent nature violate fundamental rights and Union values (Recital 28 AI Act).

### 2.1. Prohibitions Listed in Article 5 AI Act

| Provision | Prohibition | Content |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Art. 5(1)(a) | Harmful manipulation and deception | AI systems deploying subliminal techniques, purposefully manipulative or deceptive techniques, with the objective/effect of distorting behaviour, causing or reasonably likely to cause significant harm |
| Art. 5(1)(b) | Harmful exploitation of vulnerabilities | AI systems exploiting vulnerabilities due to age, disability or social/economic situation, with the objective/effect of distorting behaviour, causing or reasonably likely to cause significant harm |
| Art. 5(1)(c) | Social scoring | AI systems evaluating/classifying natural persons based on social behaviour or personal characteristics, with the social score leading to detrimental treatment in unrelated contexts or unjustified/disproportionate treatment |
| Art. 5(1)(d) | Individual criminal offence risk assessment | AI systems assessing/predicting the risk of committing a criminal offence based solely on profiling or personality traits; except to support human assessment based on objective facts linked to criminal activity |
| Art. 5(1)(e) | Untargeted scraping for facial recognition | AI systems creating or expanding facial recognition databases through untargeted scraping from the internet or CCTV footage |
| Art. 5(1)(f) | Emotion recognition | AI systems inferring emotions at the workplace or in education institutions; except for medical or safety reasons |
| Art. 5(1)(g) | Biometric categorisation | AI systems categorising persons based on biometric data to deduce/infer race, political opinions, trade union membership, religious beliefs, sex-life or sexual orientation; except for lawful labelling/filtering |
| Art. 5(1)(h) | Real-time remote biometric identification | AI systems for real-time RBI in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement; except for targeted search of victims, prevention of threats including terrorist attacks, or search of suspects of specific offences |

### 2.2. Legal Basis of the Prohibitions

(10) The AI Act is supported by two legal bases: Article 114 TFEU (internal market) and Article 16 TFEU (data protection). Article 16 TFEU serves as legal basis for prohibitions on RBI systems, biometric categorisation for law enforcement, and individual risk assessments for law enforcement. All other prohibitions are based on Article 114 TFEU.

Relevant opt-outs for Ireland (Protocol No. 21) and Denmark (Protocol No. 22) apply to the Article 16 TFEU-based prohibitions.

### 2.3. Material Scope: Placing on the Market, Putting into Service, or Use

(11) The practices prohibited relate to placing on the market, putting into service, or use of specific AI systems. For real-time RBI (Art. 5(1)(h)), only use is prohibited.

(12) **Placing on the market** (Art. 3(9)): The first making available of an AI system on the Union market -- covered regardless of means of supply (API, cloud, direct downloads, physical copies, embedded in products).

(13) **Putting into service** (Art. 3(11)): The supply of an AI system for first use to the deployer or for own use in the Union for its intended purpose -- covering both supply to third parties and in-house development.

(14) **Use** should be understood broadly to cover deployment at any moment of the lifecycle after being placed on the market or put into service, including any misuse ("reasonably foreseeable" or not) that may amount to a prohibited practice.

### 2.4. Personal Scope: Responsible Actors

(15-16) **Providers** (Art. 3(3)): Natural or legal persons that develop AI systems or have them developed and place them on the market or put them into service. Subject to AI Act if established outside the Union but place systems on the market in the Union or the output is used in the Union.

(17-18) **Deployers** (Art. 3(4)): Natural or legal persons using AI systems under their authority, unless for personal non-professional activity. Where the deployer is a legal person, individual employees acting under its control should not be considered deployers.

(19) Operators may fulfil more than one role concurrently (e.g., both provider and deployer if developing and using own system).

(20) Continuous compliance throughout all phases of the AI lifecycle is required.

### 2.5. Exclusions from Scope

#### 2.5.1. National Security, Defence and Military Purposes

(22-26) Article 2(3) AI Act: The AI Act does not apply to AI systems placed on the market, put into service, or used **exclusively** for military, defence or national security purposes. The exclusion depends on purposes, not entities. "Dual use" systems (used for both military/national security and other purposes) fall within scope of the AI Act.

National security, according to CJEU case law, refers to protecting essential State functions and fundamental interests of society, including prevention of terrorism. It does not cover road safety or administration of justice.

(27) Police and other law enforcement authorities carrying out prevention, detection, investigation and prosecution of criminal offences fall within scope of the AI Act.

(28) Activities of Europol and other Union security agencies (e.g., Frontex) fall within scope.

#### 2.5.2. Judicial and Law Enforcement Cooperation with Third Countries

(29) Article 2(4) AI Act: The AI Act does not apply to public authorities in third countries using AI systems in the framework of international cooperation agreements, provided adequate fundamental rights safeguards are ensured.

#### 2.5.3. Research and Development

(30) Article 2(8) AI Act: The AI Act does not apply to research, testing or development activity regarding AI systems prior to placing on the market or putting into service.

(31) Article 2(6) AI Act: Exclusion for AI systems specifically developed and put into service for the sole purpose of scientific research and development.

(32) Testing in real-world conditions (Art. 3(57)) is not covered by the R&D exclusion and must comply with the AI Act, unless conducted in an AI regulatory sandbox or under the special testing regime (Articles 60-61 AI Act).

#### 2.5.4. Personal Non-Professional Activity

(34) Article 2(10): The AI Act does not apply to obligations of deployers who are natural persons using systems for purely personal non-professional activity. Criminal activities are not considered purely personal. The system itself remains within scope for providers and other professional deployers.

#### 2.5.5. AI Systems under Free and Open Source Licences

(36) Article 2(12): The AI Act does not apply to AI systems released under free and open-source licences, **unless** placed on the market or put into service as high-risk systems or as systems falling under Article 5 (prohibited practices) or Article 50 (transparency obligations).

### 2.6. Interplay with High-Risk AI System Requirements

(37) AI systems classified as high-risk under Article 6/Annex III may in some cases constitute a prohibited practice if all conditions under Article 5 are fulfilled. Conversely, most AI systems falling under an Article 5 exception qualify as high-risk.

(38) AI systems not considered high-risk based on Article 6(3) may still fall within the scope of Article 5 prohibitions.

### 2.7. Application to General-Purpose AI Systems

(39) The prohibitions apply to any AI system, whether with an intended purpose or general-purpose, for direct use or integration in other AI systems.

(40) Providers are expected to take effective and verifiable measures to build in safeguards and prevent harmful behaviour and misuse to the extent reasonably foreseeable and the measures are feasible and proportionate. In their terms of use, providers are expected to exclude prohibited practices and provide appropriate instructions.

(41) In certain cases, particularly where prohibitions are linked to a very specific purpose, providers may need to primarily rely on providing appropriate instructions, restricting prohibited use, and monitoring for compliance where feasible.

### 2.8. Interplay with Other Union Law

(42) The AI Act applies horizontally across all sectors without prejudice to other Union legislation on fundamental rights, consumer protection, employment, worker protection, and product safety.

(43) Even where an AI system is not prohibited by the AI Act, its use could still be prohibited or unlawful under other Union law.

(45) The interplay with Union data protection law (GDPR, LED, EUDPR) is particularly relevant since AI systems often process personal data.

(46) For biometric categorisation and real-time RBI for law enforcement, the AI Act applies as lex specialis to Article 10 LED.

(47) EU consumer protection and safety legislation remain fully applicable.

(48-49) The AI Act applies in conjunction with the Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065) and is without prejudice to applicable liability laws.

(51) As secondary Union legislation, the AI Act must be interpreted in light of fundamental rights guaranteed by EU Treaties, the Charter, and international conventions.

### 2.9. Enforcement of Article 5

#### 2.9.1. Market Surveillance Authorities

(53) Market surveillance authorities designated by Member States (and the EDPS for EU institutions) are responsible for enforcement. They can act on own initiative or following complaints. Member States must designate competent authorities by 2 August 2025.

(54) For cross-border implications, the Union safeguard procedure applies with a Commission decision determining whether the practice is prohibited, aiming for uniform application across Member States.

#### 2.9.2. Penalties

(55) Non-compliance with Article 5 constitutes the most severe infringement, subject to the highest fine: up to EUR 35 million or 7% of total worldwide annual turnover (whichever is higher). EU institutions may face fines up to EUR 1.5 million.

(57) Since violations of Article 5 interfere the most with freedoms and give rise to the highest fines, their scope should be **interpreted narrowly**.

## Article 5(1)(a) -- Subliminal / Manipulative / Deceptive Techniques

### 3.1. Rationale and Objectives

(59) The underlying rationale is to protect individual autonomy and well-being from manipulative, deceptive, and exploitative AI practices that can subvert and impair an individual's autonomy, decision-making, and free choices. The prohibitions aim to protect human dignity (Article 1 Charter), including individual autonomy.

### 3.2. Main Components of the Prohibition

**Statutory text of Article 5(1)(a):**

> The placing on the market, putting into service or use of an AI system that deploys subliminal techniques beyond a person's consciousness or purposefully manipulative or deceptive techniques, with the objective, or the effect of materially distorting the behaviour of a person or a group of persons by appreciably impairing their ability to make an informed decision, thereby causing them to take a decision that they would not have otherwise taken in a manner that causes or is reasonably likely to cause that person, another person or group of persons significant harm.

(60) Four **cumulative conditions** must be fulfilled:
1. Placing on the market, putting into service, or use of an AI system
2. The AI system deploys subliminal, purposefully manipulative or deceptive techniques
3. Those techniques have the objective or effect of materially distorting behaviour by appreciably impairing ability to make informed decisions
4. The distorted behaviour causes or is reasonably likely to cause significant harm

(61) There must be a plausible causal link between the techniques deployed, the material distortion of behaviour, and the significant harm.

### 3.2.1. Subliminal, Purposefully Manipulative or Deceptive Techniques

#### a) Subliminal Techniques

(64) Subliminal techniques operate beyond (below or above) the threshold of conscious awareness. They bypass rational defences and influence decisions without conscious awareness.

(65) Examples of subliminal techniques (not necessarily prohibited unless all conditions fulfilled):
- **Visual subliminal messages**: Images/text flashed too quickly for conscious mind to register
- **Auditory subliminal messages**: Sounds/messages at low volumes or masked by other sounds
- **Tactile subliminal stimuli**: Subtle physical sensations perceived unconsciously
- **Subvisual and subaudible cueing**: Stimuli entirely undetectable by human senses under normal conditions
- **Embedded images**: Images hidden within other visual content
- **Misdirection**: Drawing attention to specific stimuli to prevent noticing other content
- **Temporal manipulation**: Altering perception of time in user interactions

(66) AI and related technologies (big data analytics, neuro technologies, brain-computer interfaces, virtual reality) heighten the risk of sophisticated subliminal manipulation. AI can extend to emerging machine-brain interfaces and techniques like dream-hacking and brain spyware.

#### b) Purposefully Manipulative Techniques

(67) Techniques designed or objectively aiming to influence, alter, or control behaviour in a manner that undermines individual autonomy and free choices. They typically exploit cognitive biases, psychological vulnerabilities, or situational factors. AI's adaptability enables responding to individual circumstances and increasing effectiveness at scale. **It is not necessary** that the provider/deployer intends to cause harm.

(68) The prohibition also covers techniques where individuals, even if aware of the influence attempt, may not be able to control or resist its manipulative effect (Recital 29).

(69) The prohibition covers AI systems that manipulate individuals **without any human intending them to do so** -- the AI system itself may deploy manipulative techniques learned from training data or through reinforcement learning.

By contrast, if manipulative behaviour is merely **incidental**, the system should not be considered deploying purposefully manipulative techniques, as long as the provider has taken appropriate preventive and mitigating measures.

#### c) Deceptive Techniques

(70) Techniques involving presenting false or misleading information with the objective or effect of deceiving individuals and influencing their behaviour. Covers techniques where the person is not consciously aware or, if aware, can still be deceived or cannot resist them.

(71-72) Interplay with Art. 50 transparency obligations: Labelling of deep fakes and chatbots reduces risk of deception but does not eliminate it. The prohibition has a more limited scope and may cover cases where deceptive AI-generated content presents false/misleading information that distorts behaviour.

(73) AI systems may learn deceptive techniques without human intent, including systems that identify when under evaluation and temporarily halt undesired behaviour ("evaluation gaming").

**Hallucinations by generative AI** may not constitute deceptive techniques within Art. 5(1)(a), taking into account the state of the art, particularly where the provider has properly informed users about limitations and integrated appropriate safeguards, and the system is not deployed in sensitive contexts (health, education, elections).

#### d) Combination of Techniques

(74-75) Article 5(1)(a) also applies to combinations of subliminal, manipulative, and deceptive techniques that can have compound impact, creating feedback loops.

### 3.2.2. Material Distortion of Behaviour

#### Concept of Material Distortion

(77) The concept involves deployment of techniques capable of influencing behaviour in a manner that **appreciably impairs** the ability to make an informed decision, causing individuals to behave in a way they would otherwise not have. It goes beyond minor or negligible impacts.

(78) **Appreciable impairment** refers to a substantially reduced ability to make informed and autonomous decisions. It goes beyond lawful persuasion.

(79) An **informed decision** requires understanding and knowledge of relevant information, including available options, risks and benefits, and the effects of the AI system on behaviour.

(80) For interpretation, Union consumer protection law (UCPD) is a valid source of inspiration. Per CJEU, there is no need to prove behaviour was actually distorted -- it suffices to establish the practice is "likely" (capable) of impacting the average consumer's transactional decision. Even accurate information may be misleading if presented in a way that distorts decision-making.

(81) Market surveillance authorities must investigate each case's specific facts, assessing whether the technique is likely to appreciably impair the decision-making of an "average" individual within a targeted group.

#### Scenario 1: "With the Objective to" Materially Distort Behaviour

(82) The objective may be pursued by the provider, the deployer, or the system itself within its implicit objectives. This should be distinguished from the "intended purpose" of the system.

#### Scenario 2: "With the Effect of" Materially Distorting Behaviour

(83-84) Intent is sufficient but not necessary. The prohibition also applies where no intent is present but the effect is capable of materially distorting behaviour. A plausible causal link is always necessary. Effects do not need to have fully materialised but there must be sufficient indication they are likely or capable to materialise.

### 3.2.3. Significant Harm

#### a) Types of Harms

(86-90) Main types of harms:
- **Physical harm**: Injury, damage to life, health, property. Often immediate, serious, irreversible.
- **Psychological harm**: Adverse effects on mental health and emotional well-being. May accumulate over time.
- **Financial and economic harm**: Financial loss, financial exclusion, economic instability.
- **Compound harms**: Harms often manifest in combination with broader societal harms.

#### b) Threshold for Significance

(91-93) The determination is fact-specific. Key considerations:
- **Severity of harm**: Degree of harm with objective and observable effects
- **Context and cumulative effects**: Specific context and cumulative actions
- **Scale and intensity**: Extent of harm and whether it impacts large numbers of people
- **Affected persons' vulnerability**: Children, elderly, persons with disabilities may be more susceptible; what is acceptable for adults may be unacceptable for vulnerable groups
- **Duration and reversibility**: Long-lasting or irreversible harm typically meets the threshold

#### c) Causal Link and Reasonable Likelihood

(94) The concept of "reasonably likely" allows application not only where harm has occurred but also where it is reasonably likely to occur. The assessment must consider whether the provider/deployer could have reasonably foreseen the significant harm and whether they implemented appropriate preventive and mitigating measures.

(95) To avoid providing/using prohibited systems, providers and deployers are encouraged to:
1. **Transparency and individual autonomy**: Clear disclosures, relevant information, respect for autonomy, safeguards
2. **Compliance with applicable legislation**: Compliance indicates the practice does not constitute a prohibited practice
3. **State of the art practices and industry standards**: Adherence to professional due diligence

(96) Harms resulting from factors external to the AI system and not within the control/reasonable foresight of the provider/deployer are not relevant.

### 3.3. Harmful Exploitation of Vulnerabilities (Article 5(1)(b))

**Statutory text of Article 5(1)(b):**

> The placing on the market, the putting into service or the use of an AI system that exploits any of the vulnerabilities of a natural person or a specific group of persons due to their age, disability or a specific social or economic situation, with the objective, or the effect, of materially distorting the behaviour of that person or a person belonging to that group in a manner that causes or is reasonably likely to cause that person or another person significant harm.

(98) Four **cumulative conditions**:
1. Placing on the market, putting into service, or use of an AI system
2. The AI system exploits vulnerabilities due to age, disability, or social/economic situations
3. The exploitation has the objective or effect of materially distorting behaviour
4. The distorted behaviour causes or is reasonably likely to cause significant harm

### 3.3.1. Exploitation of Vulnerabilities

(101-103) The AI system must exploit vulnerabilities inherent to certain individuals or groups due to their age, disability or specific social/economic situation. "Exploitation" means objectively making use of vulnerabilities in a manner harmful to the exploited persons, and should be distinguished from lawful practices. Exploitation may be **cumulative** (reference to "any" vulnerability).

#### a) Age

(104-105) Both young and older people. **Children** (under 18) are particularly susceptible due to developmental stage, cognitive/socio-emotional immaturity, and vulnerability to forming attachments to AI agents.

**Examples**: AI-powered toys encouraging risky challenges; games using addictive reinforcement schedules exploiting children's limited understanding of long-term consequences.

(106) **Older people** may suffer from reduced cognitive capacities, making them vulnerable to scams or coercive tactics.

#### b) Disability

(107-108) Disability encompasses long-term physical, mental, intellectual, and sensory impairments (within the meaning of Article 3(1) Directive (EU) 2019/882). AI inaccessibility is not exploitation -- systems that are simply inaccessible do not specifically target vulnerabilities.

#### c) Specific Social or Economic Situation

(109) "Specific" should not be interpreted as a unique individual characteristic but rather a legal status or membership of a specific vulnerable social/economic group. Recital 29 gives examples: persons living in extreme poverty, ethnic or religious minorities. Covers relatively stable characteristics but also transient circumstances (temporary unemployment, over-indebtedness, migration status). General grievances or loneliness are **not** covered (not specific from a socio-economic perspective), but their exploitation may fall under Article 5(1)(a).

(110-111) AI systems inadvertently biased against socially disadvantaged persons (indirect discrimination) should not automatically be considered exploitation. But providers/deployers aware of unlawful discrimination causing significant harm who have not taken corrective measures should be considered to be exploiting vulnerabilities.

### 3.3.2. Material Distortion of Behaviour (Art. 5(1)(b))

(113) Same concepts as Art. 5(1)(a). Notable difference: Art. 5(1)(b) does **not** require "appreciably impair the ability to make an informed decision" -- the vulnerabilities of children and other vulnerable persons already reduce their capacity for informed decisions.

### 3.3.3. Significant Harm (Art. 5(1)(b))

(114-120) Same concepts as Art. 5(1)(a). For vulnerable groups, harms may be particularly severe. A **precautionary approach** is warranted in case of uncertainty. Unlike Art. 5(1)(a), Art. 5(1)(b) does not explicitly refer to group harms, but Recital 29 refers to both, so the prohibitions should be interpreted consistently.

### 3.4. Interplay Between Article 5(1)(a) and (b)

(122-125) Differentiation by dominant aspect:
- If exploitation applies regardless of specific vulnerabilities: **Art. 5(1)(a)** takes precedence
- If manipulation/exploitation is targeted at a specific vulnerable group due to age, disability, or specific social/economic situation: **Art. 5(1)(b)** applies
- Exploitation of vulnerabilities of other groups (not age/disability/socio-economic) may be covered as part of Art. 5(1)(a)

### 3.5. Out of Scope

#### 3.5.1. Lawful Persuasion

(127-133) Key distinctions between manipulation and persuasion:
- **Persuasion**: Transparent, respects autonomy, presents arguments appealing to reason and emotions, explains objectives, provides accurate information, supports informed decision-making
- **Manipulation**: Typically covert, undermines autonomy, exploits psychological weaknesses or cognitive biases

Recital 29 clarifies the prohibitions do not affect:
- **Lawful medical treatment**: AI-enabled subliminal techniques in psychological treatment when carried out in accordance with applicable law and medical standards with explicit consent
- **Common commercial practices**: Advertising using AI for personalisation is not inherently manipulative if it does not deploy prohibited techniques

#### 3.5.2. Manipulative Systems Not Likely to Cause Significant Harm

(134) All manipulative, deceptive and exploitative AI applications not reasonably likely to cause significant harm are outside scope.

Examples outside scope:
- AI companionship system with anthropomorphic design that engages users but does not cause serious harms/dependency
- Therapeutic chatbot using subliminal techniques to help quit smoking (temporary discomfort outweighed by long-term benefits)
- Music platform using emotion recognition to recommend songs while avoiding depressive content
- Manipulative techniques in security training simulations (temporarily beneficial purposes without significant harms)

### 3.6. Interplay with Other Union Law

(135-145) Art. 5(1)(a) and (b) complement and are without prejudice to:
- **Consumer protection law** (UCPD): AI Act is broader in scope (protects any natural person, not just consumers)
- **Data protection law** (GDPR/LED): Compliance with transparency, data minimisation, fairness, lawfulness helps avoid harmful manipulation
- **Non-discrimination law**: Vulnerabilities due to age and disability are also protected grounds
- **Digital Services Act**: Art. 25(1) DSA prohibits dark patterns; AI Act covers broader scenarios
- **AVMSD**: Preventing harmful AI-driven advertisements in media
- **Political Advertising Regulation (EU) 2024/900**: Transparency and targeting rules
- **Product safety legislation**: Ex ante safety requirements for regulated products
- **Criminal law**: Preventing practices that may constitute or lead to criminal offences

## Article 5(1)(c) -- Social Scoring

### 4.1. Rationale and Objectives

(148) Social scoring may lead to discriminatory and unfair outcomes, exclusion from society, and social control/surveillance practices incompatible with Union values. The prohibition protects human dignity, non-discrimination, equality, data protection, private and family life, and relevant social and economic rights.

### 4.2. Main Components of the Prohibition

**Statutory text of Article 5(1)(c):**

> The placing on the market, the putting into service or the use of AI systems for the evaluation or classification of natural persons or groups of persons over a certain period of time based on their social behaviour or known, inferred or predicted personal or personality characteristics, with the social score leading to either or both of the following:
> (i) detrimental or unfavourable treatment of certain natural persons or groups in social contexts unrelated to the contexts in which the data was originally generated or collected;
> (ii) detrimental or unfavourable treatment that is unjustified or disproportionate to their social behaviour or its gravity.

Three **cumulative conditions**:
1. Placing on the market, putting into service, or use of an AI system
2. The AI system evaluates or classifies persons over a period of time based on social behaviour or personal/personality characteristics
3. The resulting social score leads to detrimental/unfavourable treatment in unrelated contexts **and/or** unjustified/disproportionate treatment

### 4.2.1. Social Scoring: Evaluation or Classification

#### a) Evaluation or Classification of Natural Persons

(151-154) The score may take various forms (mathematical number, ranking, label). "Evaluation" involves assessment/judgement; "classification" is broader and includes categorisations not necessarily involving assessment. The concept of "profiling" under data protection law (Art. 4(4) GDPR) is relevant -- the SCHUFA I judgment qualified creditworthiness scoring as profiling.

#### b) Over a Certain Period of Time

(155) The evaluation/classification must be based on data spanning a certain period -- not limited to a one-time rating. This must be assessed in context to avoid circumvention.

#### c) Based on Social Behaviour or Personal/Personality Characteristics

(156-159) **Social behaviour**: Actions, habits, interactions within society from multiple sources (participation in events, payment of debts, relations with government, etc.).

**Personal characteristics**: Sex, sexual orientation, race, ethnicity, family situation, address, income, profession, health, preferences, behaviour, location, debt level, etc.

**Personality characteristics**: Broadly synonymous with personal characteristics, may also involve creation of specific profiles.

Types of information: "Known" (provided as input, verifiable), "inferred" (derived from other information by AI), "predicted" (estimated based on patterns with less than 100% accuracy).

### 4.2.2. Detrimental or Unfavourable Treatment

#### a) Causal Link

(160-162) The treatment must be a consequence of the score. The AI output need not be the sole cause -- AI-assisted scoring combined with human assessment is covered if the AI score plays a sufficiently important role. A score produced by one organisation may lead to detrimental treatment by another.

#### b) Treatment Conditions (Alternative)

(163-164) Two alternative conditions (may also apply in combination):

**Scenario 1: Treatment in unrelated social contexts** (Art. 5(1)(c)(i))
(166) Data about social behaviour or characteristics generated/collected in contexts unrelated to the scoring context, used without apparent connection. This often happens against reasonable expectations and in violation of data protection law.

**Examples**:
- Tax authorities using unrelated data (taxpayers' social habits, internet connections) for fraud detection
- Social welfare agency using unrelated characteristics (spouse nationality, internet connection, social media behaviour) for fraud assessment
- Labour agency using unrelated variables (marital status, health data) for employment support determination

**Scenario 2: Disproportionate treatment** (Art. 5(1)(c)(ii))
(167) Treatment unjustified or disproportionate to the gravity of social behaviour. Requires case-by-case assessment considering proportionality, relevant circumstances, and ethical considerations.

**Examples**:
- Agency profiling families and removing children for minor transgressions (missing appointments, traffic fines)
- Municipality scoring trustworthiness and blacklisting residents for minor misbehaviour (unreturned library books, late tax payments)

(169) The prohibition may also cover preferential treatment given to some, since this implies less favourable treatment of others.

### 4.2.3. Regardless of Public or Private Persons

(170) The prohibition applies regardless of whether the AI system or score is provided or used by public or private persons. Private sector scoring can have similarly harmful consequences.

(171) The provider and deployer must demonstrate the AI practice is legitimate and justified, including providing transparency about functioning, data types and sources.

### 4.3. Out of Scope

(173-177) The following are **outside scope**:
- Scoring of legal entities (unless aggregating evaluation of natural persons whose behaviour was evaluated)
- Individual ratings by users assessing service quality (unless combined with AI analysis)
- Lawful evaluation practices for specific purposes in accordance with Union and national law, particularly where those laws specify relevant data and ensure justified/proportionate treatment

**Examples of legitimate scoring outside scope**:
- Financial credit scoring based on customer's income/expenses and financial circumstances, in compliance with consumer protection law
- Fraud detection based on transactional behaviour and relevant data sources
- Telematics-based insurance tariffs based on driving behaviour (proportionate to risk)
- Health data collection for medical diagnosis purposes
- Online platform profiling for safety reasons based on relevant data
- AI-targeted advertising in compliance with consumer/data protection law
- Refugee camp data used for resettlement decisions under applicable migration law
- Shopping platform scoring offering privileges based on purchase/return history
- Law enforcement evaluation using data relevant for prevention/prosecution of criminal offences

### 4.4. Interplay with Other Union Law

(178-183) Relevant legislation includes:
- **Consumer protection law (UCPD)**: Scoring may constitute unfair commercial practices
- **Data protection law**: Lawfulness, data minimisation, transparency, automated decision-making rules
- **Non-discrimination law**: Where evaluation is based on protected grounds
- **Consumer Credit Directive (EU) 2023/2225**: Creditworthiness assessment based on relevant/accurate information; prohibits special categories of personal data and social network data
- **Anti-money laundering legislation**: AI systems for AML evaluation must comply

## Article 5(1)(d) -- Criminal Offence Risk Assessment

### 5.1. Rationale and Objectives

(186) Recital 42: Natural persons should be judged on their actual behaviour and not on AI-predicted behaviour based solely on their profiling, personality traits or characteristics.

### 5.2. Main Components of the Prohibition

**Statutory text of Article 5(1)(d):**

> The placing on the market, the putting into service for this specific purpose, or the use of an AI system for making risk assessments of natural persons in order to assess or predict the risk of a natural person committing a criminal offence, based solely on the profiling of a natural person or on assessing their personality traits and characteristics; this prohibition shall not apply to AI systems used to support the human assessment of the involvement of a person in a criminal activity, which is already based on objective and verifiable facts directly linked to a criminal activity.

Three **cumulative conditions**:
1. Placing on the market, putting into service for this specific purpose, or use
2. The AI system makes risk assessments assessing/predicting risk of committing a criminal offence
3. The risk assessment is based **solely** on profiling or assessing personality traits and characteristics

### 5.2.1. Assessing Risk or Predicting Likelihood of Committing a Crime

(189-191) Risk assessments and predictions are forward-looking, concerning future criminal offences or risk of crimes at the moment (including attempts or preparatory activities). They can be made at any stage: prevention, detection, investigation, prosecution, execution of penalties, or reintegration planning.

(192) The prohibition does **not** outlaw crime prediction as such -- only where the third condition (solely based on profiling/personality traits) is met.

### 5.2.2. Solely Based on Profiling or Personality Traits

#### a) Profiling of a Natural Person

(195-196) Article 3(52) AI Act defines profiling by reference to Art. 4(4) GDPR. "Group profiling" (constructing profiles for categories of perpetrators based on historical data) is also relevant -- when applied to a specific individual, it constitutes profiling.

#### b) Assessment of Personality Traits and Characteristics

(197-198) Broad category. Recital 42 gives examples: nationality, place of birth, place of residence, number of children, level of debt, type of car (illustrative, not exhaustive).

#### c) "Solely"

(199-202) The condition "solely" means only based on profiling or personality traits. This may not be fulfilled where other real, substantial and meaningful elements are taken into account. In particular, the existence of pre-established **objective and verifiable facts** may justify the conclusion that the prohibition does not apply.

**Examples of prohibited systems**:
- Law enforcement using AI to predict terrorism solely based on age, nationality, address, car type, marital status
- Tax authorities predicting criminal tax offences solely on profile data including double nationality, number of children, opaque inferred variables
- Police assessing children's risk of "future offending" based solely on relationships/family links

### 5.2.3. Exclusion: Supporting Human Assessment Based on Objective Facts

(203-206) The prohibition does **not** apply to AI systems used to **support human assessment** of involvement in criminal activity already based on **objective and verifiable facts directly linked to criminal activity**. Where the system falls within this exclusion and is used by law enforcement, it is classified as high-risk (Annex III, point 6(d)).

Per CJEU in **Ligue des droits humains** (C-817/19): Human assessment must rely on objective criteria and ensure non-discriminatory results.

Central element: The AI system supports human assessment rather than making the risk assessment itself. The human assessment must already be based on objective and verifiable facts.

### 5.2.4. Extent to Which Private Actors Fall Within Scope

(207-211) The prohibition is not limited to law enforcement authorities. It applies to private actors:
- When entrusted by law to exercise public authority for law enforcement purposes
- When explicitly requested to act on behalf of law enforcement authorities
- When subject to legal obligations to assess/predict criminal offence risk (e.g., anti-money laundering)

However, private entities profiling customers for ordinary business operations (e.g., detecting financial irregularities to protect own interests), where risk of criminal offences is merely accidental and secondary, are **not** covered.

### 5.3. Out of Scope

#### 5.3.1. Location-Based/Geospatial Crime Predictions

(212-213) Place-based crime prediction not involving assessment of specific individuals falls outside scope. But if location-based information is linked to an individual and the system assesses the person's risk based solely on profiling (including residence in high-crime area), it becomes person-based.

#### 5.3.2. AI Systems Supporting Human Assessments Based on Objective Facts

(214) Examples outside scope:
- Profiling actual suspicious dangerous behaviour in a crowd with meaningful human assessment
- AI combining geospatial and ANPR data supporting police investigation based on verifiable facts
- AI assessing early release risk based on past offences and rehabilitation-relevant behaviour
- AI supporting pre-trial detention decisions based on valid grounds
- AI used by customs authorities assessing risk based on objective goods/supply chain information

#### 5.3.3. Crime Predictions for Legal Entities

(215-216) The prohibition applies only to natural persons. Prediction for companies/organisations is excluded. Borderline cases exist for sole traders or independent professionals.

#### 5.3.4. Predictions of Administrative Offences

(217-218) Only criminal offences are covered, excluding administrative offences. Whether an offence is criminal depends on Union or national law, subject to CJEU scrutiny based on the "Engel criteria" (classification, nature of offence, severity of penalty).

### 5.4. Interplay with Other Union Law

(219-221) Relevant legislation:
- **LED**: Art. 11(3) prohibits profiling resulting in discrimination
- **Directive (EU) 2016/343** on presumption of innocence
- National criminal procedural and police law

## Article 5(1)(e) -- Facial Recognition Database Scraping

### 6.1. Rationale and Objectives

(223) Untargeted scraping seriously interferes with rights to privacy and data protection and denies the right to remain anonymous. Recital 43 justifies the prohibition based on the "feeling of mass surveillance" and risks of "gross violations of fundamental rights."

### 6.2. Main Components of the Prohibition

**Statutory text of Article 5(1)(e):**

> The placing on the market, the putting into service for this specific purpose, or the use of AI systems that create or expand facial recognition databases through the untargeted scraping of facial images from the internet or CCTV footage.

Four **cumulative conditions**:
1. Placing on the market, putting into service for this specific purpose, or use
2. For creating or expanding facial recognition databases
3. Through AI tools for untargeted scraping
4. From the internet or CCTV footage

#### 6.2.1. Facial Recognition Databases

(226) Any collection of data organized for rapid search and retrieval, capable of matching human faces. May be temporary, centralised or decentralised. The database need not have facial recognition as its sole purpose; it is sufficient that it can be used for facial recognition.

#### 6.2.2. Untargeted Scraping

(227-231) "Scraping": Using web crawlers, bots, or other means to extract data automatically. "Untargeted": Operating like a vacuum cleaner, without targeting specifically intended subjects. Respecting robot.txt does not affect the untargeted nature.

**Targeted scraping** (collecting images only of specific individuals or pre-defined groups) is **not covered**. But step-by-step scraping selecting specific groups each time is prohibited where the end-result is functionally the same as untargeted scraping.

#### 6.2.3. From the Internet and CCTV Footage

(232-233) Publishing facial images on social media does not constitute consent for inclusion in facial recognition databases. "Reverse engineering image search engines" are considered targeted scraping.

### 6.3. Out of Scope

(234-237) Outside scope:
- Untargeted scraping of biometric data **other than** facial images (e.g., voice samples)
- Scraping where no AI systems are involved
- Facial image databases **not** used for recognition of persons (e.g., for model training where persons are not identified)
- AI systems harvesting facial images to generate images of fictitious persons
- **Existing databases** built before the prohibition's application that are not further expanded through AI-enabled untargeted scraping (but must comply with data protection rules)

### 6.4. Interplay with Other Union Law

(238) Under GDPR, LED and EUDPR, the untargeted scraping would be unlawful and no legal basis could be relied upon.

## Article 5(1)(f) -- Emotion Inference in Workplace/Education

### 7.1. Rationale and Objectives

(240-241) Emotion recognition technology is quickly evolving but faces "serious concerns about the scientific basis" (Recital 44). Key shortcomings: limited reliability, lack of specificity, limited generalisability. It can lead to discriminatory outcomes and is particularly intrusive in asymmetric relationships (workplace, education). But benefits exist for safety and medical care.

### 7.2. Main Components of the Prohibition

**Statutory text of Article 5(1)(f):**

> The placing on the market, the putting into service for this specific purpose, or the use of AI systems to infer emotions of a natural person in the areas of workplace and education institutions, except where the use of the AI system is intended to be put in place or into the market for medical or safety reasons.

Four **cumulative conditions**:
1. Placing on the market, putting into service for this purpose, or use of an AI system
2. AI system to infer emotions
3. In the area of workplace or education institutions
4. Not for medical or safety reasons (exception)

### 7.2.1. AI Systems to Infer Emotions

#### a) Emotion Recognition Systems vs. AI Systems to Infer Emotions

(244-245) Art. 3(39) defines "emotion recognition systems" as AI systems identifying/inferring emotions or intentions based on biometric data. The prohibition should be understood to include both identifying and inferring emotions or intentions, and should be limited to inferences based on biometric data.

#### b) Identification and Inference of Emotions or Intentions

(246) "Identification" occurs through direct comparison; "inferring" is done by deducing information through analytical processes.

#### c) Emotions

(247-249) The concept should be understood broadly. Recital 18 lists: happiness, sadness, anger, surprise, disgust, embarrassment, excitement, shame, contempt, satisfaction, amusement (non-exhaustive).

**Not covered**: Physical states such as pain or fatigue (including fatigue detection for pilots/drivers). Also not covered: Mere detection of readily apparent expressions, gestures or movements **unless** they are used for inferring emotions.

**Examples**:
- Observing that a person is smiling -- NOT emotion recognition
- Concluding that a person is happy -- IS emotion recognition
- Tracking how many times news presenters smile -- NOT emotion recognition
- Inferring an employee is unhappy from body gestures/frown -- IS emotion recognition
- Inferring a student is furious and about to become violent -- IS emotion recognition
- Detecting driver fatigue to suggest breaks -- NOT emotion recognition

#### d) On the Basis of Biometric Data

(250-252) Only AI systems based on biometric data constitute emotion recognition systems. Art. 3(34): Personal data resulting from specific technical processing relating to physical, physiological or behavioural characteristics. Includes facial expressions, keystroke analysis, gait, voice, EEG, ECG, eye tracking.

**Examples**:
- Inferring emotions from written text (sentiment analysis) -- NOT based on biometric data, outside scope
- Inferring emotions from keystroke, facial expressions, body postures -- IS based on biometric data, within scope

### 7.2.2. Workplace and Education Institutions

#### a) Workplace

(254) Interpreted broadly: Any physical or virtual space where persons engage in tasks assigned by employer or affiliated organisation. Includes indoor offices, factories, publicly accessible spaces (shops, stadiums), open-air sites, temporary work sites. Independent of employment status (employee, contractor, trainee, volunteer). **Also applies** to candidates during selection and hiring process.

**Examples of prohibited uses**:
- Call centre tracking employee emotions (anger) via webcams/voice recognition
- Monitoring emotional tone in hybrid work teams via video calls
- Emotion recognition during recruitment process
- Emotion recognition during probationary period
- Supermarket tracking employee emotions (happiness)

**Not prohibited under Art. 5(1)(f)**:
- Call centre tracking customer emotions (anger, impatience) to help employees cope
- Supermarket/bank detecting suspicious customers (robbery preparation) -- provided no employees are tracked

#### b) Education Institutions

(255) Broad: Public and private institutions, all levels (including vocational schools, continuous training). No limitation on types/ages or environment (online, in person, blended). Also applies to candidates during admissibility process.

**Examples of prohibited uses**:
- Education institution using emotion recognition to infer student interest/attention
- Emotion recognition during admissibility tests
- Education institution using emotion recognition on both teachers and students

**Not prohibited under Art. 5(1)(f)**:
- AI language learning app using emotion recognition **outside** an education institution
- Eye tracking software for online exams to detect unauthorized material use (not inferring emotions)
- Emotion recognition for learning purposes in role-play contexts (e.g., training actors) where results cannot impact evaluation

### 7.2.3. Exceptions for Medical and Safety Reasons

(256-263) This exception should be **narrowly interpreted**:
- **Medical reasons**: Should be understood as uses of CE-marked medical devices. Does **not** comprise general wellbeing monitoring. Detecting burnout or depression at workplace/education is **not** covered by the exception and remains prohibited.
- **Safety reasons**: Only relates to protection of life and health, **not** property (theft/fraud). Must remain limited to what is strictly necessary and proportionate, with sufficient safeguards.

**Permitted examples**: Emotion recognition to assist employees/students with autism; accessibility for blind/deaf persons.

**Prohibited despite safety framing**: Monitoring anxiety/stress levels of employees generally. Exception only applies where elevated stress would pose specific danger (e.g., operating dangerous machines, handling chemicals) -- and data may not be used for other purposes.

### 7.3. More Favourable Member State Law

(264) Art. 2(11): Member States may introduce laws more favourable to workers, e.g., prohibiting emotion recognition even for medical purposes at work.

### 7.4. Out of Scope

(265-270) Outside scope:
- AI systems inferring emotions/sentiments not based on biometric data
- AI systems inferring physical states (pain, fatigue)
- Emotion recognition in all other domains (commercial, law enforcement, public spaces) -- these are high-risk systems
- Crowd control systems not inferring individual emotions
- Medical field uses (care robots, voice monitors for emergency calls)

## Article 5(1)(g) -- Biometric Categorisation

### 8.1. Rationale and Objectives

(272) Biometric categorisation for assigning persons to groups relating to race, political/sexual orientation, etc. violates human dignity and poses significant risks to privacy and non-discrimination.

### 8.2. Main Components of the Prohibition

**Statutory text of Article 5(1)(g):**

> The placing on the market, the putting into service for this specific purpose, or the use of biometric categorisation systems that categorise individually natural persons based on their biometric data to deduce or infer their race, political opinions, trade union membership, religious or philosophical beliefs, sex-life or sexual orientation; this prohibition does not cover any labelling or filtering of lawfully acquired biometric datasets, such as images, based on biometric data or categorising of biometric data in the area of law enforcement.

Five **cumulative conditions**:
1. Placing on the market, putting into service for this purpose, or use
2. Biometric categorisation system
3. Individual persons are categorised
4. Based on their biometric data
5. To deduce or infer race, political opinions, trade union membership, religious/philosophical beliefs, sex-life, or sexual orientation

### 8.2.1. Biometric Categorisation System

(276-280) Art. 3(40): AI system for assigning natural persons to specific categories based on biometric data, **unless** ancillary to another commercial service and strictly necessary for objective technical reasons (both conditions must be cumulatively fulfilled).

"Ancillary" (Recital 16): Intrinsically linked to another commercial service, cannot be used without the principal service, and integration is not a means to circumvent the AI Act.

**Permitted examples**:
- Filters on online marketplaces allowing consumers to preview products on themselves
- Filters on social network services for adding/modifying pictures/videos

**Prohibited examples**:
- AI categorising social media users' assumed political orientation from uploaded photos for targeted political messages
- AI categorising social media users' assumed sexual orientation from photos for targeted advertising

### 8.2.2. Individual Categorisation

(281-282) Persons must be "individually" categorised. If a whole group is categorised without looking at the individual, the prohibition does not apply.

### 8.2.3. Sensitive Characteristics

(283) Only biometric categorisation to deduce or infer: race, political opinions, trade union membership, religious or philosophical beliefs, sex-life, or sexual orientation.

**Prohibited examples**:
- System claiming to deduce race from voice
- System claiming to deduce religious orientation from tattoos or faces

**Not prohibited**: System categorising persons by skin/eye colour or analysing DNA of crime victims for origin.

### 8.3. Out of Scope

(284-286) The prohibition does **not** cover labelling or filtering of lawfully acquired biometric datasets, including for law enforcement (Recital 30).

**Permitted labelling/filtering**:
- Labelling biometric data to prevent discrimination in AI training (ensuring equal representation of demographic groups)
- Categorising patients by skin/eye colour for medical diagnosis (e.g., cancer)
- Law enforcement use for labelling/filtering child sexual abuse material, identifying victims by characteristics

### 8.4. Interplay with Other Union Law

(287-288) Non-prohibited biometric categorisation systems are classified as high-risk (Annex III, point 1(b)). Art. 5(1)(g) is consistent with Art. 11(3) LED prohibiting profiling resulting in discrimination based on special categories of personal data.

## Article 5(1)(h) -- Real-Time Remote Biometric Identification

### 9.1. Rationale and Objectives

(293-294) Real-time RBI in publicly accessible spaces is highly intrusive: it may affect the private life of a large part of the population, evoke a feeling of constant surveillance, and indirectly dissuade exercise of fundamental rights. Technical inaccuracies can lead to biased/discriminatory results, particularly regarding age, ethnicity, race, sex or disabilities.

However, where strictly necessary to achieve a substantial public interest, use may be permitted under narrow exceptions with safeguards.

### 9.2. Main Components of the Prohibition

**Statutory text of Article 5(1)(h):**

> The use of 'real-time' remote biometric identification systems in publicly accessible spaces for the purpose of law enforcement, unless and in so far as such use is strictly necessary for one of the following objectives:
> (i) the targeted search for specific victims of abduction, trafficking in human beings or sexual exploitation of human beings, as well as the search for missing persons;
> (ii) the prevention of a specific, substantial and imminent threat to the life or physical safety of natural persons or a genuine and present or genuine and foreseeable threat of a terrorist attack;
> (iii) the localisation or identification of a person suspected of having committed a criminal offence, for the purpose of conducting a criminal investigation or prosecution or executing a criminal penalty for offences referred to in Annex II and punishable by a custodial sentence of at least four years.

Five **cumulative conditions**:
1. The AI system is a RBI system
2. The activity consists of use
3. In real-time
4. In publicly accessible spaces
5. For law enforcement purposes

### 9.2.1. Remote Biometric Identification

(297-299) Art. 3(41): AI system for identifying natural persons without their active involvement, typically at a distance, through comparison with a reference database. Covers only identification (not verification).

#### a) Identification Only

(300-303) Art. 3(35): Automated recognition of human features for establishing identity by comparing biometric data with database. Includes systems for following persons (e.g., tracking suspect's escape direction).

**Biometric verification** (comparing data at sensor with data on device like smartphone/passport) falls **outside** scope.

#### b) Remoteness

(304-308) Implies ability to identify without active involvement, typically at a distance. **Excluded**: Systems confirming identity for access to a service, unlocking a device, or security access (Recital 15). These have minor impact compared to RBI processing large numbers without active involvement.

Active involvement requires conscious, active approach to a camera -- being informed about camera presence is not sufficient.

RBI systems include face recognition, voice biometric technology, gait recognition, and keystroke biometric technology. Body-cams with RBI used during demonstrations fulfil the remoteness condition.

#### c) Reference Database

(309) Identification requires a reference database containing biometric data for comparison. This is indispensable.

### 9.2.2. Real-Time

(310-312) Art. 3(42): Captures and processes biometric data instantaneously, near-instantaneously, or without significant delay. All processing steps (capture, comparison, identification) occur simultaneously or almost simultaneously. A "limited short delay" is included to avoid circumvention.

A delay is significant at least when the person is likely to have left the place where biometric data was taken.

When law enforcement covertly takes a picture and submits it to a database for immediate search, depending on circumstances, this may fall under the prohibition.

### 9.2.3. In Publicly Accessible Spaces

(313-318) Art. 3(44): Any publicly or privately owned physical space accessible to an undetermined number of natural persons, regardless of conditions for access or capacity restrictions.

Characteristics:
- Accessible to undetermined number of persons (independently of tickets, registration, age)
- Irrelevance of ownership (public or private)
- No specific activity required

**Examples of publicly accessible spaces**: Concert venues (even with tickets), trade fairs, shops, restaurants, banks, gyms, stadiums, metro stations, airports, cinemas, theatres, parks, forests, playgrounds, public roads and squares.

**NOT publicly accessible spaces**:
- Online spaces (not physical)
- Factories/workplaces with entry control for employees/service providers
- Prisons and border control points
- Gated residential areas

Some spaces have dual function (e.g., airport: common areas are publicly accessible; border control area is not).

### 9.2.4. For Law Enforcement Purposes

(319-325) Art. 3(46): Activities for prevention, investigation, detection or prosecution of criminal offences or execution of criminal penalties, including safeguarding against threats to public security. Same purposes as Article 1 LED.

Covers activities by law enforcement authorities **or on their behalf**. "On behalf of" means delegation or request to other entities (including private parties) under instruction and supervision.

**Examples of "on behalf of"**: Public transport companies, sports federations, banks requested by law enforcement to act under their instructions/supervision.

Private entities acting on their own behalf for fraud detection are **not** covered by the prohibition.

### 9.3. Exceptions to the Prohibition

(326-328) Three exceptions (Article 5(1)(h)(i)-(iii)), subject to strict necessity and conditions in Article 5(2)-(7). These do not constitute a legal basis -- only domestic Member State law can allow the use.

In the absence of national legislation, use is prohibited as from 2 February 2025.

#### 9.3.1. Rationale

The objectives are: protection (victims/missing persons), prevention (imminent threats/terrorism), prosecution/investigation (suspects of serious crimes). Use must be strictly, exhaustively, and narrowly defined. Any other use is prohibited.

#### 9.3.2. Targeted Search for Victims and Missing Persons

(329-336) Art. 5(1)(h)(i): Targeted search for victims of three crimes (abduction, trafficking, sexual exploitation) and missing persons.

The search for missing children and adults differs. Adults have the right to "disappear"; a search requires concerning circumstances (health condition, legal status, suicidal note, departure without belongings). Search for missing persons under administrative (non-law enforcement) procedure falls under GDPR rules instead.

#### 9.3.3. Prevention of Imminent Threats to Life or Terrorist Attacks

(337-347) Art. 5(1)(h)(ii): Two sub-scenarios:

**a) Specific, substantial and imminent threat to life/physical safety**: Must be (1) specific (clearly defined, individualised, concrete -- not hypothetical), (2) substantial (serious bodily injuries), and (3) imminent (can occur at any moment, requires immediate action). Includes threats to critical infrastructure where disruption would result in imminent threat to life.

**b) Genuine and present or foreseeable threat of a terrorist attack**: Threshold inspired by CJEU case law on data retention (SpaceNet, C-793/19): "sufficiently concrete circumstances have arisen." May cover tracking "terrorists on the move" where there are concrete indications of a planned attack.

#### 9.3.4. Localisation and Identification of Suspects of Certain Crimes

(348-355) Art. 5(1)(h)(iii): Localisation/identification of suspects of Annex II crimes punishable by at least 4 years imprisonment.

**Annex II crimes**: Terrorism, trafficking in human beings, sexual exploitation of children, drug trafficking, weapons trafficking, murder, grievous bodily injury, trade in human organs, nuclear material trafficking, kidnapping, ICC crimes, aircraft/ship seizure, rape, environmental crime, organised/armed robbery, sabotage, participation in criminal organisation.

Does not require issuance of European Arrest Warrant. Covers both suspects and convicted perpetrators.

### Safeguards and Conditions (Article 5(2)-(7))

#### 10.1. Targeted Individual and Safeguards (Article 5(2))

(356-368) Key safeguards:
1. **Targeted deployment only**: Use only to confirm identity of specifically targeted individual -- avoiding mass surveillance
2. **Balancing assessment**: Nature of situation (seriousness, probability, scale of harm if system not used) vs. consequences for rights and freedoms
3. **Temporal, geographic and personal limitations**: Clearly limited geographic scope, duration, and targeted person
4. **Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA)** before deployment
5. **Registration in EU database** (except duly justified urgency)

##### 10.1.1. Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment

(369-376) FRIA must comply with Art. 27 AI Act and include:
- Description of RBI use, deployer's processes, intended purpose
- Period of use and frequency
- Categories of affected persons (targeted individual, persons in reference database, persons in surrounding areas)
- Specific risks of harm to affected persons
- Fundamental rights at stake (privacy, data protection, freedom of thought/expression/assembly, non-discrimination, human dignity, presumption of innocence, rights of child/elderly)
- Necessity and proportionality assessment
- Human oversight measures
- Risk mitigation measures

##### 10.1.2. Registration

(377) Deployer must register system in EU database (Art. 49). Emergency exception: deployment may start before registration if registration completed without undue delay.

#### 10.2. Prior Authorisation (Article 5(3))

(378-408) Each individual use requires prior authorisation by a judicial authority or independent administrative authority.

Key elements:
- **Double necessity/proportionality assessment**: First by deployer (FRIA), then by authorising authority
- **Reasoned request** in accordance with national procedural rules
- **"Each use"** requires individual authorisation (decisive moment is each concrete use, not installation)
- **Independence** of authorising authority: Must be independent from the authority using the system (per CJEU in Prokuratuur, C-746/18)
- **Urgency exception**: May commence without authorisation, but request must be submitted within 24 hours. If rejected, use stops immediately and all data must be immediately deleted
- **No sole automated decision-making**: No decision adversely affecting a person may be taken based solely on the RBI output
- **Human oversight** (Art. 14): Identification must be verified and confirmed by at least two natural persons with necessary competence

#### 10.3. Notification of Each Use (Article 5(4))

(409-410) Each use must be notified to the relevant market surveillance authority and national data protection authority. Notification need not include sensitive operational data.

#### 10.4. National Laws Required (Article 5(5))

(411-416) Member States may decide whether and for which objectives to authorise real-time RBI. National laws must:
- Not exceed limits set by Art. 5(1)(h)
- Specify competent authorities and authorising authorities
- Detail scope of permitted objectives
- Lay down requirements for requests, issuance, exercise, supervision and reporting
- Be notified to the Commission within 30 days of adoption
- Member States may introduce more restrictive laws

#### 10.5. Annual Reports by National Authorities (Article 5(6))

(419-422) National market surveillance and data protection authorities must submit annual reports to the Commission on use of real-time RBI systems for law enforcement.

#### 10.6. Annual Reports by the Commission (Article 5(7))

(423-424) The Commission publishes annual reports based on aggregated data, excluding sensitive operational data.

### 10.7. Out of Scope

(425-428) Outside the prohibition:
- Biometric verification/authentication systems
- Retrospective (post-) RBI systems for law enforcement (high-risk, subject to Art. 26(10))
- Real-time RBI for law enforcement in non-publicly accessible spaces (private spaces, online spaces)
- Real-time and retrospective RBI by private actors (e.g., supermarkets for shoplifters, sports arenas for banned individuals, schools for security)

All such systems remain subject to high-risk AI system rules and data protection law.

### Practical Examples

**Prohibited uses**:
- Police deploying facial recognition at football stadium entrance against unspecific watchlist not linked to the event
- Police using real-time RBI to identify shoplifters (not in Annex II)
- Police using real-time RBI in streets based on general security/crime prevention concerns (constant monitoring not meeting exception criteria)
- System automatically triggering RBI based on emotion detection at stadium (biometric system decides upon necessity)
- Police using real-time RBI against political protestor for minor criminal offence (not in Annex II; disproportionate per Glukhin v Russia ECtHR judgment)

**Not prohibited (but subject to high-risk rules)**:
- Hotels using real-time RBI to recognise VIP guests (not law enforcement)
- Shopping malls using real-time RBI for shoplifters on own behalf (not law enforcement)
- Retrospective facial recognition by police after a concert incident
- Biometric verification for nuclear plant access (active participation, not remote)
- Installation of biometric-ready CCTV cameras (installation itself not prohibited; each concrete use requires authorisation)

**Prohibited if entrusted by police**: Shopping mall using real-time RBI on police behalf to find shoplifters (law enforcement purpose, shoplifting not in Annex II exceptions).

## 11. Entry into Application

(429-431) Article 5 applies as from **2 February 2025**. Applies to all AI systems regardless of when placed on the market.

The chapters on governance and penalties apply from **2 August 2025**. In the interim period (Feb-Aug 2025), prohibitions are fully applicable and mandatory even without market surveillance authorities or penalty provisions. The prohibitions have **direct effect** and can be enforced in national courts.

## 12. Review and Update

(432-433) These Guidelines constitute a first interpretation. The Commission will review them as necessary in view of practical experience, technological developments, enforcement actions, and CJEU interpretations.
